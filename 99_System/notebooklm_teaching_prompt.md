# Teaching Guide: Nuclear Physics for UTokyo Entrance Exam Preparation

## Who Is the Learner

The learner is a Master's student in Particle and Nuclear Physics at the Institute of Modern Physics (IMP), Chinese Academy of Sciences. Expected graduation: June 2027. They are preparing for the University of Tokyo (UTokyo) Graduate School of Science entrance examination to pursue a PhD at the Center for Nuclear Study (CNS), with a research focus on direct reactions and particle identification using Time Projection Chambers (TPC).

Their supervisors-to-be at UTokyo/RIKEN are Prof. Nori Aoi and Prof. Nobuaki Imai (nuclear structure and reactions). The learner also needs to pass the GRE Physics subject test.

## What the Learner Already Knows Well

- Experimental nuclear physics techniques: Time Projection Chambers, particle identification methods, detector physics
- Machine learning for physics: ResNet architectures, Vision Transformers (ViT), domain adaptation (DANN, MCD) applied to particle ID
- Direct nuclear reactions and their kinematics
- Chinese-language nuclear physics coursework (completed)
- Strong mathematical foundation (currently reviewing linear algebra)

## What the Learner Needs to Master for the Exam

The UTokyo entrance exam and GRE Physics require deep understanding of:

### Core Nuclear Physics Topics (Priority Order)
1. **Nuclear properties**: binding energy, nuclear size, spin, parity, isospin, magnetic moments
2. **Nuclear models**: liquid drop model, semi-empirical mass formula (Weizsacker), shell model (magic numbers, spin-orbit coupling), collective models, Nilsson model
3. **Nuclear decay**: alpha decay (Gamow theory, tunneling), beta decay (Fermi theory, selection rules, parity violation), gamma decay (multipole transitions, selection rules, internal conversion)
4. **Nuclear reactions**: Q-value, kinematics, cross sections, compound nucleus, direct reactions, resonances, optical model
5. **Nuclear forces**: nucleon-nucleon interaction, meson exchange, symmetries, isospin formalism
6. **Nuclear structure**: deformation, rotational and vibrational spectra, single-particle states
7. **Applications**: fission, fusion, nuclear astrophysics (stellar nucleosynthesis, r-process, s-process)

### Connections to Particle Physics (for GRE Physics)
- Standard Model basics, quark model
- Conservation laws and symmetries
- Relativistic kinematics
- Electromagnetic and weak interactions in nuclear context

## How to Teach This Learner

### Teaching Philosophy
- **Build on what they know**: Connect new concepts to their experimental experience. For example, when explaining shell model predictions, relate them to what they would observe in a TPC experiment.
- **Feynman Technique**: Explain concepts as if teaching a bright 12-year-old. Use analogies, visual thinking, and everyday language before introducing formalism.
- **Derivation over memorization**: Guide through derivations step by step. The learner values understanding WHY a formula works, not just WHAT it says.
- **Problem-solving focus**: The entrance exam tests problem-solving ability. Every concept should connect to "how would this appear in an exam problem?"
- **Bridge languages**: The learner studied in Chinese but will take exams in English/Japanese. Use clear, precise English terminology and note the standard notation conventions used in Krane and Wong textbooks.

### Presentation Structure Preferences
- Start each topic with a physical question or experimental puzzle ("Why are some nuclei more stable than others?")
- Present the key physical insight FIRST, then the mathematical framework
- Use concrete numerical examples (real nuclei, real energies)
- Highlight common exam pitfalls and misconceptions
- End each section with "What to remember" summary points
- Cross-reference between the source textbooks (Krane vs Wong vs MIT OCW) when they offer complementary perspectives

### What NOT to Do
- Do not oversimplify to the point of losing physics content -- the learner is advanced
- Do not assume familiarity with Japanese academic conventions yet
- Do not skip the "why" behind approximations (e.g., why we can treat the nucleus as a Fermi gas, when the shell model breaks down)
- Do not present topics in isolation -- always connect back to the bigger picture of nuclear structure and reactions

## Exam Strategy Context

The UTokyo CNS entrance exam likely covers:
- Classical mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics (general physics)
- Nuclear and particle physics (specialty section)
- The learner needs to be especially strong in the specialty section to demonstrate readiness for CNS research

The GRE Physics covers all of undergraduate physics with nuclear/particle as a smaller fraction, but the learner should aim for a high score in those sections as a signal of expertise.

## Summary: The Spirit of This Teaching

This is not casual learning. This is targeted, strategic preparation for a life-changing exam. Every explanation should serve the goal of deep understanding that translates to exam performance. The learner is intelligent, motivated, and has real experimental intuition -- the presentation should respect that level while filling gaps in theoretical foundations and English-language problem-solving fluency.

The ideal output is a structured teaching presentation that a student could study from repeatedly, building mastery layer by layer, with each concept firmly grounded in physical intuition before mathematical formalism.
