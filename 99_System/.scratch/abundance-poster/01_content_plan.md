## 2. Poster Structure -- Section-by-Section Outline

### COLUMN 1 (left, ~32% width): "Setting the Stage"

#### Block 1.1: Title Bar (spans all columns)
- **Title:** Chemical Abundance Evolution in the Universe
- **Subtitle line:** Nuclear Astrophysics
- **Author:** [Student name], Institute of Modern Physics, CAS

#### Block 1.2: Introduction -- Why Abundances Matter
**Heading:** "Cosmic Chemical Inhomogeneity"

Key content (3 bullets):
- The composition of the Universe is NOT homogeneous -- nucleosynthesis is a gradual, ongoing process whose efficiency depends on the local astrophysical environment.
- The Milky Way shows a clear metallicity gradient: the bulge and inner disk are metal-rich; the halo is metal-poor -- reflecting different star-formation histories.
- Elements are built through successive astrophysical sites: Big Bang (H, He, Li), stellar interiors (C to Si), core-collapse supernovae (Si to Ni), AGB stars (s-process heavy elements), and neutron star mergers (r-process heavy elements).

**Scope:** This poster traces the chemical abundance evolution from Big Bang to present day, focusing on the Milky Way and solar neighborhood.

**Image:** Include Image A (Galaxy structure diagram) here, sized to ~80% column width. Caption: "Structure of the Milky Way. (a) Face-on view showing spiral arms and Sun's position. (b) Edge-on view showing disk, bulge, and metal-poor halo. Disk:Halo star ratio ~ 10:1."

#### Block 1.3: Metallicity as a Cosmic Clock
**Heading:** "Stellar Populations and the Age-Metallicity Relation"

Key content (3 bullets):
- A star's metallicity records its birth epoch. The iron-to-hydrogen ratio, $[\text{Fe/H}] = \log((\text{Fe/H})_\star / (\text{Fe/H})_\odot)$, is the standard scale.
- **Pop I** (disk, [Fe/H] ~ 0, young) vs. **Pop II** (halo, [Fe/H] < -2, old) vs. **Pop III** (primordial, zero metals, predicted but unobserved).
- Metallicity correlates with age on average, but the scatter at any epoch is enormous (>2 dex), reflecting stochastic local enrichment.

**Image:** Include Image B ([Fe/H] vs. Age scatter plot) here, sized to ~80% column width. Caption: "Stochastic enrichment model: [Fe/H] vs. stellar age. Mean metallicity rises steeply then saturates, but scatter spans >2 dex at any epoch (Argast et al. 2000)."

### COLUMN 2 (center, ~36% width): "The Four Stages" (Centerpiece)

#### Block 2.1: Four Stages of Abundance Build-Up
**Heading:** "Four Stages of Chemical Abundance Evolution"

Introductory sentence: The build-up of chemical elements proceeds through four broadly recognizable stages, each leaving a characteristic imprint on the abundance pattern (mass fraction vs. mass number A).

**Stage 1 -- Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (t ~ 3 min):**
- Only H (~75% by mass), He-4 (~25%), and traces of D, He-3, Li-7 are produced.
- The abundance pattern has only ~5 data points spanning 10 orders of magnitude.

**Stage 2 -- First Stellar Generations (Oldest Halo Stars, [Fe/H] < -3):**
- Core-collapse supernovae inject C, N, O (hydrostatic burning) and iron-group elements (explosive Si burning).
- The Fe peak at A~56 emerges as the dominant new feature; elements beyond A~60 are still absent.

**Stage 3 -- Early r-Process Enrichment:**
- Neutron-rich ejecta (neutron star mergers or rare magnetorotational CCSNe) produce elements beyond the Fe peak via rapid neutron capture.
- Sparse, irregular features appear at A~80-100 and near the r-process peaks at A~130 (N=82 shell closure) and A~195.

**Stage 4 -- Present Day (Solar System Abundances):**
- After ~9 Gyr of Galactic chemical evolution, the full solar abundance curve is established with ~200+ measured species.
- The s-process peaks (A~88 Sr, A~138 Ba, A~208 Pb) -- built up by AGB stars over ~0.1-10 Gyr timescales -- fill in the gaps between the r-process peaks, completing the heavy-element pattern absent in Stage 3.
- Type Ia supernovae, detonating on ~1 Gyr delay timescales, contribute additional Fe-peak elements and drive the characteristic decline of [alpha/Fe] toward solar values.

**Images:** Include Images C, D, E, F as a 2x2 grid (the four-panel evolutionary sequence), each at ~46% of the column width. This is the visual centerpiece of the poster. Label each panel:
- Top-left (C): "Stage 1: Big Bang -- only H, He, Li"
- Top-right (D): "Stage 2: Old Halo Stars -- Fe peak emerges"
- Bottom-left (E): "Stage 3: Early r-process -- heavy elements appear"
- Bottom-right (F): "Stage 4: Today (Sun) -- full abundance curve"

Caption beneath the grid: "Evolution of the abundance pattern from Big Bang to present day. Each panel plots mass fraction vs. mass number A. Arrows indicate temporal progression. (Source to be verified; cf. Frebel & Norris, ARAA 2012.)"

**Transition to Column 3:** The four stages above describe *what* was produced and *when*. To identify the responsible mechanisms and constrain their timescales, we turn to element-ratio diagnostics.

### COLUMN 3 (right, ~32% width): "Evidence and Models"

#### Block 3.1: Observational Evidence -- [X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H]
**Heading:** "Observational Signatures: Element Ratios Across Metallicity"

Key content (4 bullets):
- Stellar spectroscopy measures [X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] for ~20 elements (C through Zn), providing a fossil record of nucleosynthesis history.
- **Alpha-elements (O, Mg, Si, Ca, Ti):** Plateau at [X/Fe] ~ +0.4 for [Fe/H] < -1 (pure CCSN enrichment, corresponding to Stages 2-3), then decline toward solar as SN Ia Fe contributions set in -- the "knee" at [Fe/H] ~ -1 marks the transition to Stage 4.
- **Iron-peak elements (Cr, Mn, Co, Ni, Zn):** Mn and Co show opposite trends with metallicity, constraining the relative SN Ia vs. CCSN contributions.
- The alpha-element knee is a clock: it dates the onset of SN Ia contributions to ~1 Gyr after the start of star formation.

**Image:** Include Image G ([X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] multi-panel plot) here, sized to ~85% column width. Note: at poster scale each sub-panel will be ~65 mm wide. Consider cropping to show only the alpha-elements (O, Mg, Si, Ca, Ti) if legibility is an issue at 1-meter distance. Caption: "[X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] for elements C through Zn. Black points: observations. Red/blue curves: models with/without AGB. Note the alpha-element plateau at low [Fe/H] and knee near [Fe/H] ~ -1 (Nomoto et al. 2013)."

#### Block 3.2: Theoretical Supernova Yield Models
**Heading:** "Supernova Yields: Theory vs. Observation"

Key content (3 bullets):
- CCSNe yields for 15, 20, 25 M_sun progenitors are computed at primordial (Z=0) and solar (Z=0.02) metallicity by three independent groups (Heger & Woosley 2010; Limongi & Chieffi 2006/2012; Tominaga, Umeda & Nomoto 2007).
- At solar metallicity all three models converge, reproducing [X/Fe] ~ 0 for most elements. Alpha-elements are robustly overproduced, consistent with the observed plateau.
- At zero metallicity models diverge dramatically, especially for odd-Z elements -- primordial nucleosynthesis and the nature of the first stars remain major open questions.

**Image:** Include Image H ([X/Fe] vs. Z six-panel grid) here, sized to ~85% column width. Caption: "[X/Fe] vs. atomic number for 15, 20, 25 M_sun progenitors at Z=0 (left) and Z=0.02 (right). Three models compared. Large discrepancies at Z=0 highlight open questions (Nomoto et al. 2013)."

#### Block 3.3: Conclusions
**Heading:** "Conclusions"

Key content (4 concise bullets):
- Chemical abundances are built over cosmic time through successive stellar generations, from H/He/Li to all 92 natural elements.
- Diagnostic signatures -- the Fe peak (A~56), s-process peaks (A~88, 138, 208), and r-process peaks (A~130, 195) -- fingerprint distinct nucleosynthesis mechanisms.
- The alpha-element plateau and knee in [X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] directly constrain the relative timescales of CCSNe (~Myr) vs. SNe Ia (~Gyr).
- Yield models reproduce solar-metallicity patterns well, but large model discrepancies at Z=0 show that primordial nucleosynthesis remains an open frontier.

## 3. Narrative Flow

The poster is designed to be read **left-to-right, top-to-bottom**:

1. **Column 1 (top) -- "Why are abundances inhomogeneous?"** The reader starts with the fundamental observation and the Galaxy structure image (Image A) as concrete context. A scope statement frames the poster's coverage.

2. **Column 1 (bottom) -- "How do we quantify this?"** The [Fe/H] scale and Pop I/II/III classification, with the age-metallicity scatter plot (Image B) showing that metallicity correlates with age but with enormous scatter.

3. **Column 2 (center, full height) -- "How did the elements build up?"** The four-stage sequence (Images C-F in a 2x2 grid) is the visual centerpiece. Each stage is annotated with 2 short bullets. The s-process contribution is explicitly placed in Stage 4, closing the narrative loop opened by the introduction. A transition sentence at the bottom bridges to Column 3.

4. **Column 3 (top) -- "What does observation tell us?"** The [X/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] plot (Image G) shows the fossil record. The alpha-element plateau and knee are explicitly connected back to the four stages (Stages 2-3 = plateau; Stage 4 onset = knee).

5. **Column 3 (middle) -- "Can we model this?"** The yield comparison (Image H) shows model convergence at solar Z and divergence at Z=0, leading to open questions.

6. **Column 3 (bottom) -- "What have we learned?"** Conclusions tie the story together. References and acknowledgments close the poster.

**Visual flow cues:** The four-stage images have built-in gray arrows guiding the reader through the temporal sequence. The left column sets up "why"; the center answers "how"; the right provides "evidence and verification."
