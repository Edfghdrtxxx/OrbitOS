### 1. Blue arrow claim is partially correct but oversimplified
- File: 01_physics_audit.md
- Issue: The audit states the blue arrow "should point at the repulsive core, not the attractive well" (line 55, recommendation 1 on line 79). Looking at the figure, the blue arrow indeed points into the attractive well region. The audit's correction is directionally right — the high-momentum SRC tail is most strongly associated with the short-range repulsive core — but the reasoning is incomplete. SRC do not arise exclusively from the repulsive core. The tensor force, which operates at intermediate range (the outer part of the attractive well, ~0.7-1.4 fm), is actually the *dominant* source of SRC in nuclear matter, particularly for np pairs in the deuteron-like (T=0, S=1) channel. The audit itself acknowledges this in its "Conceptual concerns" section (line 75) but contradicts it in the main recommendation by implying the blue arrow should point *only* at the repulsive core. A more accurate correction would be: the blue arrow should point at the short-range region generally (both the repulsive core and the inner attractive well), not exclusively at the repulsive core. If the figure intends the green arrow for the tensor/intermediate-range part and the blue arrow for the hard-core part, then the audit's recommendation is correct — but it should state this interpretation explicitly.
- Why it matters: If the implementer moves the blue arrow to point exclusively at the hard core and ignores the tensor contribution, the figure becomes a different kind of inaccurate — it would imply SRC are purely from central repulsion, which contradicts the last two decades of SRC research (Subedi et al. 2008, Hen et al. 2014, etc.).
- Confidence: High

### 2. Wavefunction node-counting claim needs qualification
- File: 01_physics_audit.md
- Issue: The audit claims (lines 29-30) that the deeper state showing more nodes than the shallower state is "physically backwards." This is stated as a general rule but is only correct *for states of the same orbital angular momentum l*. The audit does hedge with "clarify that they correspond to different l values" but presents the swap as the primary recommendation. In a realistic nuclear shell model, one commonly shows states like 1s (0 nodes) and 1p (0 radial nodes but one more oscillation from the angular part's effect on the radial wavefunction shape). The deeper state in the figure has two peaks and the shallower state has one peak. In a Woods-Saxon potential, for the same l, the ground state (1s, 1p, 1d...) is nodeless and higher-n states have more nodes; and the ground state is the most deeply bound. So if both wavefunctions have the same l, the audit is correct that the depiction is inverted. However, if they represent different l values (e.g., the deeper one is 1s or 2s and the shallower one is 1p), then the node structure could be correct. The figure does not label the quantum numbers, so the audit should have been more careful to present both possibilities rather than asserting "this is incorrect." The recommendation to "swap the wavefunctions" as the primary fix could itself introduce an error if the original intent was to show different l states.
- Why it matters: An implementer following the recommendation blindly could swap wavefunctions that were intentionally drawn for different l values, creating a new physics error.
- Confidence: Medium-high. The audit's physics reasoning is sound for same-l states, but the assertion of error is premature without knowing the intended quantum numbers.

### 3. The blue curve in rho(p) is NOT a plateau — the audit misreads the figure
- File: 01_physics_audit.md
- Issue: The audit claims (line 45) that the blue curve in rho(p) shows "a plateau or rise" between 2-3 fm^-1 and calls this "a significant physics inaccuracy." Looking at the actual figure, the blue curve *does* rise from very low values at low momentum (~1 fm^-1) up to a visible level around 2-3 fm^-1, and then curves over near 3-4 fm^-1. This rising behavior at lower momenta is not unphysical — it is the SRC *component* of the momentum distribution emerging from below the mean-field contribution. The blue curve represents the SRC contribution alone (not the total distribution), so it is expected to rise from negligible values, peak somewhere around 2-2.5 fm^-1 (where SRC begin to dominate), and then fall off at higher momenta. The ~1/k^4 Tan contact behavior applies to the *total* momentum distribution tail at asymptotically large k, not necessarily to the shape of the isolated SRC component over the 2-4 fm^-1 range shown. Furthermore, the exact 1/k^4 scaling is derived from contact formalism in cold atoms and has been adapted to nuclear systems, but the nuclear case is more complex due to the tensor force and finite-range interactions — the power law is approximate. That said, the blue curve in the figure does appear to still be rising or flat near 3 fm^-1 with no clear turnover, which is problematic. The audit's core point is valid — the SRC tail should eventually decrease — but the characterization of the issue as a "plateau or rise" where monotonic decay is expected misidentifies the problem. The issue is that the turnover point is not visible within the plotted range, not that the curve should be monotonically decreasing from 2 fm^-1 onward.
- Why it matters: If the implementer makes the blue curve monotonically decreasing from 2 fm^-1, the resulting figure would incorrectly show the SRC component as always decreasing, when in fact it should rise from negligible values, peak, and then fall. The correction should specify: the blue curve should peak around 1.5-2 fm^-1 and then show clear falloff through 3-4 fm^-1, not be monotonically decreasing over the entire displayed range.
- Confidence: High on the physics; medium on the exact figure reading (the curve colors blend slightly).

### 6. MISSED ISSUE: The "n-n interaction" label is restrictive
- File: 01_physics_audit.md
- Issue: The figure labels the potential as "n-n interaction" (neutron-neutron). SRC physics is dominated by neutron-proton (np) pairs, not nn pairs, due to the tensor force operating primarily in the T=0 (isospin singlet) channel. The np/pp SRC ratio is approximately 20:1 from JLab experiments (Subedi et al. 2008, Hen et al. 2014). If the figure is meant to illustrate the origin of SRC in general, labeling it "n-n" is misleading. The audit does not flag this. It should be "NN interaction" or "nucleon-nucleon interaction" to be general. Alternatively, if the context is specifically about nn correlations (e.g., in neutron-rich matter), then the label is acceptable but the SRC tail would be much smaller than for np pairs.
- Why it matters: This is a conceptual accuracy issue. A figure about SRC that only references nn interactions misrepresents which channel dominates.
- Confidence: High
