## Summary

A collection of practical methods for moving from B2 to C1 English — the stage where grammar is mostly correct but output still sounds "translated" rather than native. The core shift at this level is not learning more rules, but developing **register awareness**, **clause-linking fluency**, and **collocational accuracy**.

## The Core Insight

At B2, your ideas and logical structure are already there. What is missing is the surface polish: how native speakers glue clauses together, which words naturally pair with each other, and how register (formal vs. informal) shapes word choice. This gap closes through deliberate noticing and output practice — not more grammar textbooks.

## Methods

### 1. Collect Register-Stacked Synonym Pairs

Do not memorize vocabulary lists. Instead, when you encounter a word in reading, stack it by register:

| Neutral | Formal/Academic |
|---|---|
| solve | address, circumvent, mitigate |
| show | demonstrate, illustrate, reveal |
| use | employ, utilize, leverage |
| get | obtain, acquire, derive |
| help | facilitate, enable, aid |

Write one sentence using each register variant. Your wiki notes and Feynman blocks are a natural place to practice — try writing one explanation in conversational tone and one in formal register.

### 2. Master Clause-Linking Patterns

The biggest shift from B2 to C1 is not vocabulary — it is how you connect ideas across clauses. A few high-yield upgrades:

| B2 (choppy) | C1 (fluid) |
|---|---|
| X is short. So it decays. | X is so short **that** it decays before… |
| Even X. But still Y. | **Although** X, Y **nonetheless**… |
| We do X. The reason is Y. | We do X **in order to** Y |
| X happened. Then Y happened. | X happened, **thereby** triggering Y |
| X is true. Also Y is true. | **Not only** is X true, **but** Y as well |
| I think X. But maybe not. | X **appears to** be the case, **though** further… |

Pick one pattern per week. Force yourself to use it in writing (daily notes, wiki entries, emails) until it becomes automatic.

### 3. Read One Paper Abstract Per Day — Out Loud

Not to understand the physics — you already do. Read it to hear the rhythm. Academic English has a predictable cadence: **hedge → claim → evidence → qualification**. After a few weeks your sentences will start falling into that shape naturally.

Reading aloud builds prosodic memory — your mouth learns the rhythm before your conscious mind can analyze it.

### 4. Sentence Mining from Papers

When you read a paper and a sentence strikes you as well-written, copy it into a collection. Then:

1. Identify the **structural template** (e.g., "By employing X, we demonstrate that Y, thereby enabling Z")
2. **Replace the content** with your own topic, keeping the structure
3. After 20–30 templates, you will start generating academic sentences naturally

This is more effective than grammar exercises because you are learning real patterns from real usage, not artificial examples.

### 5. The Shadowing Technique

Listen to academic lectures, conference talks, or podcasts (e.g., physics colloquia, TED talks). Repeat what the speaker says **immediately** after them, mimicking their intonation, stress, and rhythm — not just the words.

This builds:
- Natural prosody (stress and intonation patterns)
- Automaticity with common academic phrases
- Listening precision (you cannot shadow what you cannot hear)

Start with short segments (30 seconds). Increase as it becomes easier.

### 6. Register-Switching Exercise

Take a single idea and rewrite it in three registers:

> **Casual:** The nucleus basically forgets how it got excited — doesn't matter if a neutron hit it or if you snuck one in via a deuteron.
>
> **Neutral:** The compound nucleus loses memory of how it was formed, so the decay probabilities are the same regardless of the formation mechanism.
>
> **Academic:** Under the Weisskopf-Ewing approximation, the decay branching ratios of the compound nucleus are independent of the formation channel, provided the excitation energy is sufficiently high.

This trains your brain to decouple **what you mean** from **how you say it** — the fundamental skill of register control.

### 7. Learn Collocations, Not Isolated Words

Many B2 errors are not grammar mistakes — they are collocation mismatches. English words have preferred partners:

| Unnatural | Natural |
|---|---|
| do a mistake | **make** a mistake |
| strong tea / strong rain | strong tea / **heavy** rain |
| say a speech | **give** a speech |
| very loose binding | **loosely bound** |
| overcome the problem | **address** the problem / **overcome** the difficulty |

Use a collocations dictionary (Oxford Collocations Dictionary or Macmillan) when writing. Over time, the correct pairs become intuitive.

### 8. Write Fast, Then Revise for Register

Do not aim for perfection in first drafts. Write quickly in your natural voice, then do a single revision pass focused on register. Ask yourself:
- Can I replace any "and/but/so" with a more precise connector?
- Are there any casual words that should be upgraded for this context?
- Do my sentences vary in length, or are they all short and choppy?

This is how native speakers write formal text too. Nobody produces polished academic prose on the first try.

### 9. The "Noticing Hypothesis" in Practice

Linguist Richard Schmidt showed that acquisition requires **conscious noticing** — you do not absorb language passively. When reading papers or listening to talks, actively notice:
- How do they introduce a contrasting idea? (*However*, *Nevertheless*, *On the other hand*)
- How do they hedge a claim? (*appears to*, *is likely to*, *suggests that*)
- How do they signal structure? (*First*, *Furthermore*, *In summary*)

Highlight or write these down. The act of noticing is itself the learning mechanism.

### 10. Output Journaling with Delayed Self-Correction

Write freely for 10 minutes each day in English — about anything (physics, daily life, ideas). The next day, reread what you wrote with fresh eyes and correct it yourself before checking with any tool. This builds your **internal editor**, which is the real difference between B2 and C1.

## Application

These methods map directly to [[English_Learning]] (TOEFL writing and speaking) and to the academic writing required for [[Fundamental_Knowledge]] wiki notes. The wiki itself is a built-in practice environment — every note you write is an opportunity to apply these techniques.

**Minimum viable daily routine:**
1. Read one abstract out loud (2 min)
2. Write one wiki entry or daily log paragraph in English (10 min)
3. Revise yesterday's writing for register (5 min)

Total: ~17 minutes/day. Consistency beats intensity.
