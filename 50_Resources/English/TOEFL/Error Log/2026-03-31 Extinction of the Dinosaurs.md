## Passage: Extinction of the Dinosaurs

Topic: The K-Pg mass extinction event, iridium anomaly in boundary clay, and the asteroid impact hypothesis.

---

## Error 1 — Sentence Simplification

> **Highlighted sentence:** "Many plants and animals disappear abruptly from the fossil record as one moves from layers of rock documenting the end of the Cretaceous up into rocks representing the beginning of the Cenozoic (the era after the Mesozoic)."

**Your answer:** Fossils from the Cretaceous period of the Mesozoic up to the beginning of the Cenozoic era have been removed from the layers of rock that surrounded them.

**Correct answer:** The fossil record suggests that there was an abrupt extinction of many plants and animals at the end of the Mesozoic era.

### Why wrong

The word **"disappear"** here carries a **stative** meaning — "are no longer present" — describing the *absence* of fossils in newer rock layers. But "disappear" can also convey an **active/agentive** meaning — "were made to vanish / were taken away." You read it the active way, which naturally led to choosing an answer about fossils being physically "removed" from rock.

The context resolves the ambiguity: "disappear **from the fossil record** as one moves from [older layers] up into [newer layers]" describes an **observation** (a scientist scanning layers and noticing absence), not an event (something extracting fossils). The subject is "plants and animals," not their fossils — they disappeared from the record because they went extinct, not because their fossils were removed.

Your chosen answer also introduces a fabricated idea ("removed from the layers of rock") that is nowhere in the original sentence — a hallmark of TOEFL distractor traps.

### Takeaway

- **"Disappear"** has two possible readings: stative ("are no longer present") vs. active ("were taken away"). In academic/scientific writing, it almost always means the former. Resolve the ambiguity by checking: is the sentence describing an *observation* or an *event*?
- **Impersonal "one":** In "as **one** moves from layers of rock...", "one" is the formal impersonal pronoun meaning "a person in general / anyone." It's equivalent to informal "you" — the sentence imagines a generic observer scanning geological layers. This usage is common in academic English: "One might argue...", "One can observe...". Don't confuse it with the number or a back-reference to a specific noun.
- For Sentence Simplification: the correct answer preserves the **core meaning** (what happened + when). Reject any option that introduces a new concept not present in the original.

## Error 2 — Inference

> **Question:** Paragraph 5 implies that a special explanation of the Ir in the boundary clay is needed because...

> [!info]- Original passage (Paragraph 5)
> Ir has not been common at Earth's surface since the very beginning of the planet's history. Because it usually exists in a metallic state, it was preferentially incorporated in Earth's core as the planet cooled and consolidated. Ir is found in high concentrations in some meteorites, in which the solar system's original chemical composition is preserved. Even today, microscopic meteorites continually bombard Earth, falling on both land and sea. By measuring how many of these meteorites fall to Earth over a given period of time, scientists can estimate how long it might have taken to deposit the observed amount of Ir in the boundary clay. These calculations suggest that a period of about one million years would have been required. **However**, other reliable evidence suggests that the deposition of the boundary clay could not have taken one million years. **So** the unusually high concentration of Ir seems to require a special explanation.

**Your answer:** the Ir in the boundary clay was deposited much more than a million years ago

**Correct answer:** the amount of Ir in the boundary clay is too great to have come from microscopic meteorites ==during the time the boundary clay was deposited==

### Why wrong

**Two factors led to the wrong choice:**

**Factor 1 — Grabbed a detail instead of the conclusion.** The passage builds a logical chain:

1. Normal meteorite Ir accumulation would take ~1 million years to produce the observed amount.
2. **But** other evidence shows the boundary clay could NOT have taken 1 million years to form.
3. **Therefore** ("So"), the Ir must have a different (special) source.

You latched onto the "million years" figure as the answer, but the passage uses that number only to set up the **contradiction** — the real point is the *mismatch* between the Ir quantity and the available deposition time.

**Factor 2 — Misread the correct answer as too "absolute."** You rejected the correct answer because "too great to have come from microscopic meteorites" seemed to claim that *none* of the Ir came from meteorites. But the critical qualifier is at the end: **"during the time the boundary clay was deposited."** With that qualifier, the statement means:

- Meteorites *did* contribute some Ir (nobody denies that).
- But within the *actual deposition time* of the boundary clay, meteorites alone *couldn't account for the full amount*.
- It's a **quantitative mismatch** ("too great for the time available"), not a categorical denial ("none came from meteorites").

The lesson: in long TOEFL answer options, **end-of-sentence qualifiers can completely change the meaning**. Read the full option before judging whether it's too strong.

### Takeaway

- **Track the argument's logical flow**, not just individual facts. Look for signal words: "However," "So," "suggests that" — these mark where the author pivots from evidence to conclusion.
- For Inference questions: the answer is usually the **logical conclusion** the paragraph builds toward, not a restated detail.
- **Read answer options to the very end.** Qualifiers like "during the time..." at the tail of a long option can turn an "absolute" claim into a perfectly reasonable one. Don't reject an option based on the first half alone.

## Error 3 — Vocabulary

> **Word:** "disruption" — *"This **disruption** of the food chain and climate would have eradicated the dinosaurs and other organisms in less than fifty years."*

**Your answer:** exhaustion

**Correct answer:** disturbance

### Why wrong

- **Disruption** = an interruption or disturbance to the normal functioning of something.
- **Exhaustion** = the state of being completely used up or depleted.

You likely associated "exhaustion" with the *consequences* of the disruption (resources running out, species dying), but the word "disruption" itself describes the **act of disturbing/interrupting** a system, not depleting it. Context confirms: the asteroid impact *disturbed* the food chain and climate, it didn't *use them up*.

### Takeaway

- Match the word's meaning, not its thematic association. Ask: **can I swap the word into the sentence and keep the same meaning?**
  - "This *disturbance* of the food chain..." works perfectly.
  - "This *exhaustion* of the food chain..." changes the meaning entirely.

## Blind Spot Analysis

### 1. Word-level ambiguity + complex sentence parsing

Multiple layers of difficulty compounded in Error 1:

- **"Disappear"** — stative ("are no longer present") vs. active ("were taken away"). You read it as active, leading to "removed from rock." In academic writing, it almost always means the former.
- **Impersonal "one"** — "as *one* moves from layers of rock..." uses the formal generic pronoun (= "anyone / a person in general"), not the number. This is common in academic English but easy to misparse.
- **Dense prepositional structure** — "from layers of rock documenting X up into rocks representing Y" packs multiple modifiers into one clause, obscuring the core meaning.

**Action:**
- When verbs like *disappear, emerge, surface, decline, vanish* appear in academic passages, ask: observation or event?
- Watch for impersonal "one" — it signals a generic/hypothetical observer, not a specific agent.
- For long, complex sentences, **strip to the core clause first** (Subject + Verb + Object), then layer modifiers back in. Here: "Plants and animals disappear from the fossil record" — everything else is detail about *where* and *when*.

### 2. Logical chain tracking (argument structure)

In Error 2, you grabbed a detail instead of following the full argument. TOEFL Reading passages frequently present evidence, then pivot with "However/But/So" to reach a conclusion. You need to identify **which part of the chain the question is asking about**.

**Action:** When you see an Inference question, mentally outline the paragraph's argument:
- Evidence A says X
- **But** evidence B contradicts X
- **So** the conclusion is Y
- The answer is almost always Y, not X.

### 3. Vocabulary — associative guessing vs. substitution testing

For vocab questions, you're choosing words that *feel related to the topic* rather than words that *mean the same thing*. This is a common trap.

**Action:** Always use the **substitution test** — plug each option into the original sentence and check if the meaning stays the same. If the sentence changes its message, that option is wrong regardless of how thematically related it seems.

### 4. TOEFL distractor trap — passage word recycling

A pattern across Errors 1 and 2: both wrong answers **borrow exact words or numbers from the passage** to seem plausible.

- Error 1: your wrong choice reused "layers of rock" — passage phrasing repurposed in a fabricated claim.
- Error 2: your wrong choice reused "million years" — a specific number from the passage attached to a wrong conclusion.

This is a deliberate TOEFL distractor strategy. Wrong answers that *sound* like the passage are designed to catch test-takers who recognize familiar words without verifying meaning.

**Action:** Treat passage-word recycling as a **yellow flag**, not a green light. When an answer option echoes exact phrasing from the passage, slow down and verify: does it preserve the passage's *meaning*, or just its *words*?

### 5. Skipping end-of-sentence qualifiers in long answer options

In Error 2, you rejected the correct answer as "too absolute" because "too great to have come from microscopic meteorites" sounded like it denied *any* meteorite contribution. But the tail-end qualifier — **"during the time the boundary clay was deposited"** — turns it from a categorical denial into a quantitative mismatch. You judged the option based on its first half and never let the qualifier reshape your reading.

Long TOEFL options are designed this way: the opening phrase sets a strong claim, and a trailing qualifier narrows it to something defensible. Skipping the qualifier makes correct answers look wrong.

**Action:** Force yourself to **read every answer option to its last word** before accepting or rejecting it. If an option seems too extreme, check whether a qualifier at the end softens it.
