# Mayer 1948 — On Closed Shells in Nuclei

## Paper Summary

Maria Goeppert Mayer's landmark 1948 paper systematically compiles experimental evidence that nuclei with **20, 50, 82, or 126 neutrons or protons** form particularly stable closed-shell configurations. This paper laid the empirical groundwork for the [[Nuclear Shell Model]].

## Main Argument

While the Hartree model already predicted a closed shell at 20 nucleons (Ca-40), Mayer demonstrates through multiple independent lines of evidence that 50, 82, and 126 are equally significant shell closures — a fact that was "not generally recognized" at the time.

## Evidence Presented

### I. Isotopic Abundances
- For heavy even-Z elements, no single isotope normally exceeds 60% abundance — except at N = 50 (Sr-88: 82%) and N = 82 (Ba-138: 72%, Ce-140: 90%)
- The lightest isotopes normally have <2% abundance — exceptions cluster at N = 50 and N = 82

### II. Number of Isotones
- N = 82 has **7 stable isotones** (the maximum in the entire periodic table)
- N = 50 has **6 stable isotones** (second highest)
- Average for even N is only 3-4 isotones

### III. Stability Curve Edges
- At N = 50 and N = 82, the limits of stability for both neutron-rich and neutron-poor isotopes stay at constant neutron number — unique behavior

### IV. Proton Shells at Z = 20 and Z = 50
- Ca (Z = 20): unusually wide spread of isotopes (8 mass numbers)
- Sn (Z = 50): the most isotopes of any element (10), with a 12-neutron spread

### V. Z = 82 and N = 126
- Pb (Z = 82) is the endpoint of all radioactive decay chains
- Pb-208 (Z = 82, N = 126) is doubly magic
- α-decay energies show a sharp dip below N = 126, indicating ~2.2 MeV discontinuity in neutron binding energy

### VI. Absolute Abundance
- Cosmic abundance peaks at N = 50 (Zr), Z = 50 (Sn), N = 82 (Ba), and Z = 82/N = 126 (Pb)

### VII. Delayed Neutron Emitters
- Kr-87 (N = 51) and Xe-137 (N = 83) — nuclei just past closed shells — are the only identified delayed neutron emitters, consistent with weak binding of the extra neutron

### VIII. Low Absorption Cross Sections
- Nuclei at N = 50, 82, 126 have anomalously low neutron absorption cross sections (Y-89 is ~20-30× lower than neighbors)

### IX. Asymmetric Fission
- U-235 has 143 neutrons = 82 + 50 + 11; fission fragments tend to have ≥82 or ≥50 neutrons

### X. Binding Energy Estimates
- Semi-empirical mass formula analysis yields a discontinuity of ~2 MeV at N = 82 — about 30% of the average neutron binding energy in that region

## Historical Significance

This paper was a crucial precursor to the full shell model. One year later (1949), Mayer and independently Jensen introduced the [[Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei|spin-orbit coupling]] that explained *why* these numbers are magic — leading to the 1963 Nobel Prize in Physics.
