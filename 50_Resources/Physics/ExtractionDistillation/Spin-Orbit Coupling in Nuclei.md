# Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei

## Definition

Spin-orbit (SO) coupling in nuclear physics is the interaction between a nucleon's orbital angular momentum and its intrinsic spin. It splits single-particle energy levels into j = l + 1/2 and j = l - 1/2 substates. This splitting is essential for reproducing the observed [[Nuclear Magic Numbers]] beyond 20.

## Key Points

- Introduced phenomenologically by Mayer and Jensen (1949) to explain the magic numbers 28, 50, 82, 126
- The nuclear SO force is **much stronger** than the atomic SO interaction and has the **opposite sign** — the j = l + 1/2 state is pushed *down* in energy (more bound)
- This large splitting pushes high-j orbitals (e.g., 1f₇/₂, 1g₉/₂) into the shell below, creating the shell gaps at 28, 50, 82, 126
- Despite its success, the **microscopic origin** of nuclear SO coupling remains an open question
- Modern understanding connects it to the tensor component of the nucleon-nucleon interaction and three-nucleon forces (3NF) derived from chiral effective field theory
- Ab initio calculations with chiral NN+3N forces successfully reproduce SO splittings, including the smallest SO-originated magic number at Z = 6

## The Z = 6 Case

The 1p shell splits into 1p₃/₂ (j = 3/2) and 1p₁/₂ (j = 1/2). Filling the 1p₃/₂ subshell with 4 protons (on top of the 1s₁/₂ closed shell of 2 protons) gives Z = 6 — the smallest proton number where SO coupling creates a [[Subshell Closure at Z=6|subshell closure]].
