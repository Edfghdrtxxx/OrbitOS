# Nuclear Shell Model

## Definition

The nuclear shell model describes atomic nuclei as systems where nucleons (protons and neutrons) move nearly independently in an average potential created by all other nucleons. Nucleons fill discrete energy levels (shells), and completed shells produce particularly stable configurations — the [[Nuclear Magic Numbers]].

## Key Points

- Proposed independently by Maria Goeppert Mayer and J. Hans D. Jensen (1949, Nobel Prize 1963)
- The basic idea is analogous to the electron shell model of atoms, but with crucial differences in the relevant forces
- A simple mean-field potential (harmonic oscillator or Woods-Saxon) predicts shell closures at 2, 8, 20 — but fails to reproduce 28, 50, 82, 126
- Adding a strong [[Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei|spin-orbit coupling]] term splits energy levels and correctly reproduces all observed magic numbers
- Modern ab initio approaches use chiral effective field theory (nucleon-nucleon + three-nucleon forces rooted in QCD) to derive the shell structure from first principles

## Shell Structure

The ordering of single-particle levels (with spin-orbit splitting) produces the familiar shell gaps:

- **1s, 1p** → closures at 2, 8
- **1d₅/₂, 2s₁/₂, 1d₃/₂** → closure at 20
- **1f₇/₂** → closure at 28
- **2p₃/₂, 1f₅/₂, 2p₁/₂, 1g₉/₂** → closure at 50
- Higher shells → closures at 82, 126

Subshell closures (e.g., at the 1p₃/₂ level producing Z = 6) can also generate local magic behavior in specific regions of the nuclear chart.
