# Nuclear Magic Numbers

## Definition

Magic numbers are specific numbers of protons or neutrons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) at which atomic nuclei exhibit exceptional stability. Nuclei with magic numbers of nucleons form "closed shells" analogous to the closed electron shells of noble gases in atomic physics.

## Key Points

- The classical magic numbers are **2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126** — nuclei with these nucleon counts have extra binding energy, more stable isotopes, and anomalously low neutron absorption cross sections
- The first three (2, 8, 20) are explained by the simple harmonic oscillator or square-well potential (Hartree model)
- The numbers 28, 50, 82, 126 require the inclusion of [[Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei]] to explain — this was the breakthrough of Mayer (1949) and Jensen
- Evidence for magic numbers comes from multiple independent observables: isotopic abundances, number of stable isotones, neutron separation energies, delayed neutron emission, absorption cross sections, and the asymmetry of fission fragments
- Unlike atomic closed shells (where ionization energy varies by hundreds of percent), nuclear shell closures produce binding energy discontinuities of ~30% — yet their effects are still very pronounced

## Subshell Closures

Beyond the classical magic numbers, **subshell closures** at smaller nucleon numbers can create local magic behavior. The smallest spin-orbit-originated magic number is the [[Subshell Closure at Z=6]], observed in neutron-rich carbon isotopes.

## Key Evidence (from Mayer 1948)

| Observable | Magic Number Evidence |
|---|---|
| Isotopic abundance anomalies | Concentrated at N = 50, 82 |
| Maximum stable isotones | 7 at N = 82; 6 at N = 50 |
| Maximum isotopes per element | Sn (Z = 50) has 10 isotopes — the most of any element |
| End of radioactive chains | Pb (Z = 82, N = 126) |
| Delayed neutron emitters | Kr-87 (N = 51) and Xe-137 (N = 83) — just past closed shells |
| Low absorption cross sections | At N = 50, 82, 126 |
