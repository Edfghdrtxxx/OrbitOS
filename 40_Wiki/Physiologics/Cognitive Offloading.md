# Cognitive Offloading

## Definition

Cognitive offloading is the act of externalizing mental content — tasks, worries, plans, unresolved thoughts — into a physical medium (paper, notes, apps) to reduce the brain's active cognitive load. The brain maintains "open threads" for anything it fears forgetting; writing them down signals that the thread can be safely closed.

## Key Points

- Rooted in the **Zeigarnik effect**: the mind preferentially remembers incomplete tasks. Offloading marks them as "captured," releasing the mental hold
- Most effective as a **pre-sleep ritual** — dumping open loops before bed directly counters [[Pre-sleep Hyperarousal]]
- Three components for a complete offload: (1) what happened today, (2) what's on your mind, (3) one priority for tomorrow
- The act of writing is the intervention, not the output — even if you never re-read the notes, the brain benefits from the externalization
- Works best as a **shutdown trigger**: the offload *is* the signal that the workday is over, not something done after

## Examples

- An evening review section in a daily note: "What got done? What's on my mind? One priority for tomorrow?" — then laptop closes
- Writing tomorrow's task list before bed significantly reduced sleep onset latency in a controlled study (Scullin et al., 2018)
