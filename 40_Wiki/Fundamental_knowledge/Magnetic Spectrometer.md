# Magnetic Spectrometer

## Definition

A magnetic spectrometer is a device that uses magnetic fields to analyze charged particles by bending their trajectories according to their [[Magnetic Rigidity]] $B\rho = p/q$, enabling momentum determination and [[Particle Identification]]. In nuclear and particle physics, magnetic spectrometers are essential for measuring reaction products in scattering experiments and for [[Isotope Separation]] of [[Radioactive Isotope Beam]] species.

## Key Points

- Core principle: a charged particle with momentum $p$ and charge $q$ follows a curved path of radius $\rho$ in a magnetic field $B$, so measuring $\rho$ (via position at the focal plane) yields $p/q$
- Consist of combinations of [[Dipole Magnet]]s (for bending/dispersion) and [[Quadrupole Magnet]]s (for focusing) — the arrangement defines the [[Ion Optics]] and resolving power
- [[Focal Plane Detector]]s (e.g., [[Time Projection Chamber]], [[Scintillation Detector]], position-sensitive detectors) at the dispersive focus measure particle position, angle, energy loss, and timing for complete event reconstruction via [[Track Reconstruction]]
- [[BigRIPS]] at RIKEN is an in-flight separator and spectrometer using superconducting magnets and an [[Achromatic Degrader]] to produce and identify exotic nuclei event-by-event
- [[SHARAQ Spectrometer]] achieves high momentum resolution ($p/\Delta p \sim 15000$) for missing-mass spectroscopy in direct reactions at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)]]

## Examples

- Grand Raiden at [[RCNP]] is a high-resolution magnetic spectrometer used for $(p,p')$ and $(d,d')$ reactions, achieving $\Delta E \sim 30$ keV resolution
- The S800 spectrograph at NSCL/FRIB uses a combination of dipoles and quadrupoles to measure momentum and angles of reaction products from fast [[Radioactive Isotope Beam]] experiments
