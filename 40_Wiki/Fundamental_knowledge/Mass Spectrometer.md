# Mass Spectrometer

## Definition

A mass spectrometer is an instrument that measures the mass-to-charge ratio ($m/q$) of ions by exploiting differences in their trajectories under electromagnetic fields. In nuclear physics, mass spectrometers determine atomic masses with high precision, providing critical input for [[Binding Energy]] calculations and nuclear structure studies.

## Key Points

- Measurement principle: ions are separated by $m/q$ using electric fields, magnetic fields, or time-of-flight differences — the [[Magnetic Rigidity]] $B\rho = mv/q$ is a fundamental quantity relating momentum to the applied field
- **Storage-ring techniques** are widely used for exotic nuclei: [[Isochronous Mass Spectrometry]] (IMS) exploits revolution-period differences, while [[Schottky Mass Spectrometry]] (SMS) measures frequency of cooled ions — both operate at facilities like the CSRe at [[Institute of Modern Physics]]
- [[Penning Trap]] mass spectrometers achieve the highest precision ($\delta m / m \sim 10^{-9}$) by measuring the cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = qB/m$ of a single stored ion
- In-flight mass identification at [[BigRIPS]] combines [[ΔE-E Method]] energy loss with [[Time-of-Flight]] and $B\rho$ measurement for event-by-event [[Particle Identification]]
- Precision mass values reveal [[Shell closure]] effects, constrain the [[Liquid Drop Model]], and provide input for [[r-process Nucleosynthesis]] calculations

## Examples

- The CSRe storage ring at IMP performs IMS of short-lived nuclei produced by [[Projectile Fragmentation]], achieving mass uncertainties of ~10 keV
- ISOLTRAP at CERN uses a [[Penning Trap]] to measure masses of [[Radioactive Isotope Beam]] species produced via [[ISOL]]
