# Isobutane

## Definition

Isobutane ($\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$, specifically 2-methylpropane) is a hydrocarbon gas widely used as a fill or quench gas in gaseous particle detectors such as [[Time Projection Chamber]]s. Its high stopping power and favorable [[Drift Velocity]] characteristics make it a common choice for tracking charged particles in nuclear and particle physics experiments.

## Key Points

- Acts as a **quench gas** that absorbs UV photons from avalanche processes, suppressing secondary discharges and improving detector stability
- Provides relatively **low [[Diffusion]]** and well-controlled drift properties, yielding good [[Spatial Resolution]] for track imaging
- Typical operating pressures in active-target TPCs range from tens to ~100 torr, depending on the reaction kinematics and desired energy loss ($\text{d}E/\text{d}x$, see [[dE-dx]] and [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]])
- Often used in active-target detectors where the gas simultaneously serves as both the detection medium and the reaction target

## Examples

- In the AT-TPC at FRIB, isobutane at ~57.7 torr and ~98.1 torr has been used for [[Direct Reactions]] measurements, enabling visual event scanning ("eye scan") of particle tracks
- Mixed with other gases (e.g., He + isobutane) to tune stopping power and drift properties for specific experimental needs
