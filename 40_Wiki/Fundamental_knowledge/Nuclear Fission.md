# Nuclear Fission

## Definition

Nuclear fission is a process in which a heavy atomic nucleus splits into two (or rarely more) lighter fragments of comparable mass, accompanied by the emission of neutrons and a large release of energy ($\sim 200\;\text{MeV}$ per event). The energy release arises from the difference in [[Mass Defect|binding energy per nucleon]] between the heavy parent and the lighter products, as described by the [[B_A curve|$B/A$ curve]].

## Key Points

- **Liquid-drop picture:** the [[Liquid Drop Model]] explains the fission barrier as a competition between the nuclear surface energy (resists deformation) and the [[Coulomb Barrier|Coulomb repulsion]] (drives elongation); fission becomes energetically favourable for heavy nuclei with large $Z^2/A$
- **Fissility parameter:** $x = \frac{E_{\text{Coulomb}}}{2\,E_{\text{surface}}} \propto Z^2/A$; for $x \gtrsim 1$ the barrier vanishes and the nucleus is unstable against spontaneous fission
- **Induced fission:** can be triggered by neutron absorption (thermal or fast), [[Coulomb Excitation]], photon absorption (photofission), or transfer reactions — the compound nucleus is formed above the fission barrier
- **Neutron-rich products:** the parent's high $N/Z$ ratio is distributed among fragments, making fission a prolific source of neutron-rich isotopes; exploited in [[In-Flight Fission]] for [[Radioactive Isotope Beam|radioactive beam]] production
- **Mass distribution:** typically asymmetric for actinides (heavy peak $A \sim 140$, light peak $A \sim 95$) due to shell effects near $Z = 50$, $N = 82$

## Examples

- **Thermal-neutron fission of ${}^{235}\text{U}$:** the basis of nuclear power reactors; a slow neutron is captured, forming ${}^{236}\text{U}^*$ above the fission barrier, which splits into two fragments plus $\sim 2.4$ prompt neutrons
- **In-flight fission of ${}^{238}\text{U}$ at RIKEN:** high-energy uranium beams undergo nuclear fission in a thin Be target, producing intense neutron-rich beams separated by [[BigRIPS]]
