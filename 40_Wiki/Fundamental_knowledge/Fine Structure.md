# Fine Structure

## Definition

Fine structure is the splitting of atomic [[Spectral Lines]] into closely spaced components due to [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]] and relativistic corrections. The energy correction scales as $\alpha^2$ (where $\alpha \approx 1/137$ is the fine-structure constant), lifting the degeneracy of states with the same $n$ but different total [[Angular Momentum]] $j$.

## Key Points

- The dominant contribution is [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]]: the magnetic field seen by the electron in its rest frame interacts with the electron spin, splitting levels by $j = l \pm 1/2$
- A relativistic kinetic energy correction (deviation from non-relativistic $p^2/2m$) also contributes at the same order in $\alpha^2$
- The combined fine-structure formula for hydrogen is $\Delta E_{fs} = -\frac{E_n\,\alpha^2}{n}\left(\frac{1}{j+1/2} - \frac{3}{4n}\right)$
- States with the same $n$ and $j$ but different $l$ remain degenerate in the Dirac equation — this is broken by the Lamb shift (a QED effect)
- The fine-structure constant $\alpha = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar c)$ sets the scale of electromagnetic interactions

## Examples

- The hydrogen $2p$ state splits into $2p_{3/2}$ and $2p_{1/2}$, with $2p_{3/2}$ at slightly higher energy — the splitting is about $4.5 \times 10^{-5}$ eV
- The sodium D-line doublet (589.0 nm and 589.6 nm) arises from fine-structure splitting of the $3p$ level
