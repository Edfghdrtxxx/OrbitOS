# Superposition

## Definition

**Superposition** is the principle that if $|\psi_1\rangle$ and $|\psi_2\rangle$ are valid quantum states, then any [[Linear Combination|linear combination]] $\alpha|\psi_1\rangle + \beta|\psi_2\rangle$ (with $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}$) is also a valid state. This follows directly from the [[Vector_Spaces|vector space]] structure of quantum state spaces.

## Key Points

- Superposition is a consequence of the state space being a [[Vector_Spaces|vector space]] (specifically a [[Hilbert Space]])
- The coefficients $\alpha, \beta$ are [[Probability Amplitude|probability amplitudes]] — their squared moduli give measurement probabilities
- Measurement "collapses" the superposition to a single [[Basis|basis]] state
- Classical systems do not exhibit superposition because their state spaces are not vector spaces

## Examples

- Spin-1/2: $|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\uparrow\rangle + \beta|\downarrow\rangle$ with $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$
- Double-slit experiment: a particle passes through both slits simultaneously, with amplitudes that interfere
