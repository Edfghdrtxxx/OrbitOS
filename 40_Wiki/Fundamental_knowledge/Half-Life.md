# Half-Life

## Definition

Half-life ($t_{1/2}$) is the time required for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. After one half-life, 50% of the original [[Radioisotope]] atoms remain; after two half-lives, 25%; and so on exponentially.

## Key Points

- Mathematically: $N(t) = N_0 \cdot (1/2)^{t/t_{1/2}}$, or equivalently $N(t) = N_0 \, e^{-\lambda t}$ where $\lambda = \ln 2 / t_{1/2}$ is the decay constant
- Ranges from fractions of a second (e.g., $^{8}\text{Be}$, $\sim 10^{-16}\;\text{s}$) to billions of years (e.g., $^{238}\text{U}$, $4.5 \times 10^{9}$ years)
- Critically important for radiation safety: short half-life materials become safe quickly but are intensely radioactive initially; long half-life materials persist as low-level hazards
- The [[ALARA Principle]] leverages half-life — scheduling maintenance after activated components have decayed reduces worker dose

## Examples

- $^{24}\text{Na}$ ($t_{1/2} = 15$ hours): activated aluminum near a cyclotron beamline decays to safe levels within a few days
- $^{60}\text{Co}$ ($t_{1/2} = 5.27$ years): activated steel components remain radioactive for decades and require shielded storage
