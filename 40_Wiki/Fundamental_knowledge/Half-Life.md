# Half-Life

## Definition

Half-life (t₁/₂) is the time required for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. After one half-life, 50% of the original [[Radioisotope]] atoms remain; after two half-lives, 25%; and so on exponentially.

## Key Points

- Mathematically: N(t) = N₀ · (1/2)^(t/t₁/₂), or equivalently N(t) = N₀ · e^(−λt) where λ = ln2/t₁/₂ is the decay constant
- Ranges from fractions of a second (e.g., ⁸Be, ~10⁻¹⁶ s) to billions of years (e.g., ²³⁸U, 4.5 × 10⁹ years)
- Critically important for radiation safety: short half-life materials become safe quickly but are intensely radioactive initially; long half-life materials persist as low-level hazards
- The [[ALARA Principle]] leverages half-life — scheduling maintenance after activated components have decayed reduces worker dose

## Examples

- ²⁴Na (t₁/₂ = 15 hours): activated aluminum near a cyclotron beamline decays to safe levels within a few days
- ⁶⁰Co (t₁/₂ = 5.27 years): activated steel components remain radioactive for decades and require shielded storage
