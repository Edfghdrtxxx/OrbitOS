# Synchrotron

## Definition

A synchrotron is a circular particle accelerator in which both the guiding magnetic field and the RF accelerating frequency increase synchronously with the particle's momentum, keeping the orbit radius essentially fixed. This allows the use of a narrow vacuum chamber and smaller magnets compared to a [[Cyclotron]], enabling acceleration to very high (relativistic) energies.

## Key Points

- The orbit radius is held constant; bending magnets ramp in time so that $B\rho = p/q$ is always satisfied at the design radius, where $p$ is the relativistic momentum
- RF cavities provide energy gain each revolution; the RF frequency tracks the increasing revolution frequency (or is held constant once particles are ultra-relativistic, $v \approx c$)
- [[Phase Stability]] (synchrotron oscillations) keeps particles bunched — particles arriving early/late relative to the synchronous phase gain slightly less/more energy and self-correct
- Typical examples: CERN LHC (protons up to $6.5\;\text{TeV}$), J-PARC Main Ring, HIRFL-CSR at [[Institute of Modern Physics]]
- Synchrotrons can also serve as [[Synchrotron Radiation]] sources when electrons radiate X-rays in the bending magnets (e.g., SPring-8, operated by the RIKEN SPring-8 Center)

## Examples

- **Heavy-ion research:** The CSR (Cooler-Storage Ring) complex at [[Institute of Modern Physics|IMP]] accelerates heavy ions for nuclear structure and astrophysics experiments
- **Medical therapy:** Proton/carbon synchrotrons (e.g., HIMAC in Chiba, Japan) deliver beams for cancer treatment with precise [[Bethe-Bloch Formula|energy-loss]] depth control
