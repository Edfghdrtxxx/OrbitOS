# Fermi Energy

## Definition

The Fermi energy $E_F$ is the highest occupied single-particle energy level of a system of non-interacting fermions at absolute zero ($T = 0$). It arises from the [[Pauli Exclusion Principle]]: fermions fill available quantum states one per state from the lowest energy upward, and $E_F$ marks the top of this filled "Fermi sea."

## Key Points

- In the free [[Fermi Gas Model]] (3D), $E_F = \dfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}(3\pi^2 n)^{2/3}$, where $n$ is the number density and $m$ the particle mass
- At $T = 0$ all states with $E < E_F$ are occupied and all states with $E > E_F$ are empty; at finite $T$ the occupation follows the [[Fermi-Dirac Distribution]] with a smooth transition near $E_F$
- In nuclear physics, the Fermi energy of nucleons inside a nucleus is typically $\sim 33$–$38\;\text{MeV}$, setting the scale for the depth of the [[Mean-Field Potential]] (the nuclear potential well is roughly $E_F + |B/A| \approx 40$–$50\;\text{MeV}$ deep, where $|B/A|$ is the average [[B_A curve|binding energy per nucleon]])
- Closely related to but distinct from the [[Chemical Potential]] $\mu$: at $T = 0$, $\mu = E_F$ exactly; at finite $T$ they differ
- The [[Density of States]] $g(E) \propto E^{1/2}$ (in 3D) determines how quickly levels fill and therefore the value of $E_F$

## Examples

- **Metals:** Conduction electrons in copper have $E_F \approx 7.0\;\text{eV}$, far above thermal energy $k_BT \approx 0.025\;\text{eV}$ at room temperature — explaining why most electrons do not contribute to heat capacity
- **Nuclear matter:** Neutrons and protons in a heavy nucleus each fill their own Fermi seas; the neutron Fermi energy exceeds the proton one in neutron-rich nuclei, driving beta-decay and neutron skin formation
