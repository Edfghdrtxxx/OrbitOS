# Gamma Ray

## Definition

A Gamma Ray is a high-energy photon emitted during [[Nuclear Transition|nuclear de-excitation]], [[Radioactive Decay|radioactive decay]], or particle annihilation, typically carrying energies from tens of keV to several MeV — it is the most penetrating form of [[Ionizing Radiation]].

## Key Points

- **Origin**: Produced when a nucleus transitions from an excited state to a lower energy state, releasing the energy difference as a discrete photon; also generated in positron–electron annihilation (511 keV)
- **Interaction mechanisms**: [[Photoelectric Absorption]] (dominant at low E), [[Compton Scattering]] (intermediate E), and [[Pair Production|pair production]] (E > 1.022 MeV) govern how gamma rays deposit energy in matter — all three shape the response of detectors like [[HPGe Detector|HPGe]] and [[Scintillation Detector|scintillators]]
- **Detection**: Measured via [[Gamma Spectroscopy]] using high-[[Energy Resolution]] detectors; the [[Pulse Height]] of the resulting electrical signal is proportional to the deposited energy
- **Shielding**: Attenuated exponentially by dense materials (lead, tungsten); quantified by the mass attenuation coefficient

## Examples

- ⁶⁰Co emits two cascade gamma rays at 1.173 MeV and 1.332 MeV, commonly used for detector calibration and [[Coincidence Detection]] experiments
- In nuclear medicine, the 140 keV gamma ray from ⁹⁹ᵐTc is imaged by a scintillation camera for diagnostic imaging
