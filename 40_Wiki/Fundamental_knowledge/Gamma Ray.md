# Gamma Ray

## Definition

A Gamma Ray is a high-energy photon emitted during [[Nuclear Transition|nuclear de-excitation]], [[Radioactive Decay|radioactive decay]], or particle annihilation, typically carrying energies from tens of keV to several MeV — it is the most penetrating form of [[Ionizing Radiation]].

## Key Points

- **Origin**: Produced when a nucleus transitions from an excited state to a lower energy state, releasing the energy difference as a discrete photon; also generated in positron–electron annihilation ($511\;\text{keV}$)
- **Interaction mechanisms**: [[Photoelectric Absorption]] (dominant at low $E$), [[Compton Scattering]] (intermediate $E$), and [[Pair Production|pair production]] ($E > 1.022\;\text{MeV}$) govern how gamma rays deposit energy in matter — all three shape the response of detectors like [[HPGe Detector|HPGe]] and [[Scintillation Detector|scintillators]]
- **Detection**: Measured via [[Gamma Spectroscopy]] using high-[[Energy Resolution]] detectors; the [[Pulse Height]] of the resulting electrical signal is proportional to the deposited energy
- **Shielding**: Attenuated exponentially by dense materials (lead, tungsten); quantified by the mass attenuation coefficient

## Examples

- $^{60}\text{Co}$ emits two cascade gamma rays at $1.173\;\text{MeV}$ and $1.332\;\text{MeV}$, commonly used for detector calibration and [[Coincidence Detection]] experiments
- In nuclear medicine, the $140\;\text{keV}$ gamma ray from $^{99m}\text{Tc}$ is imaged by a scintillation camera for diagnostic imaging
