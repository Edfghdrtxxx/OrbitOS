# Fine Structure Constant

## Definition

The fine structure constant $\alpha$ is the dimensionless coupling constant that characterizes the strength of the electromagnetic interaction between charged particles, defined as:

$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0\,\hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}$$

## Key Points

- Being dimensionless, $\alpha$ is independent of the unit system — its value is a pure number that any civilization could measure.
- It governs perturbative expansions in quantum electrodynamics (QED): each additional photon vertex in a Feynman diagram contributes a factor of $\alpha$, making QED remarkably convergent.
- It sets the scale of atomic fine structure splittings (spin-orbit coupling), the hydrogen energy levels receiving corrections of order $\alpha^2$, and the Lamb shift at order $\alpha^3$.
- In nuclear physics, $\alpha$ enters through the [[Coulomb Barrier]] height and the Sommerfeld parameter $\eta = Z_1 Z_2 \alpha / \beta$, which controls [[Rutherford Scattering Formula|Rutherford scattering]] and tunneling probabilities.
- The running of $\alpha$ with energy scale (from $\approx 1/137$ at low energy to $\approx 1/128$ at the $Z$ boson mass) is a key prediction of QED and input for electroweak unification.

## Examples

- The hydrogen $2P_{3/2}$–$2P_{1/2}$ fine structure splitting is $\propto \alpha^2 \cdot 13.6\,\text{eV} \approx 4.5 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{eV}$.
- The anomalous magnetic moment of the electron $a_e = (g-2)/2 \approx \alpha/(2\pi)$ provides the most precise test of QED, agreeing with experiment to better than 1 part in $10^{10}$.
