# Relativity theory

## Definition

Relativity theory is the framework developed by Albert Einstein describing how the laws of physics relate between observers in different states of motion. It comprises [[Special Relativity]] (1905), governing physics in [[Inertial Reference Frame|inertial frames]] at constant velocity, and [[General Relativity]] (1915), which extends the theory to accelerating frames and describes gravity as the curvature of [[Spacetime]].

## Key Points

- **Principle of relativity:** The laws of physics are identical in all inertial reference frames — no experiment can distinguish absolute rest from uniform motion
- **Invariance of $c$:** The speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of their relative motion or the motion of the source
- **[[Mass-Energy Equivalence]]:** The relation $E = mc^2$ shows that mass and energy are interchangeable, with profound consequences in nuclear and particle physics (see [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]], [[Mass Defect]])
- **Time dilation and length contraction:** Moving clocks run slower and moving objects contract along the direction of motion, described by the [[Lorentz Transformation]]
- **Curved spacetime:** In [[General Relativity]], massive objects curve [[Spacetime]], and free-falling objects follow geodesics in that curved geometry

## Examples

- The [[Photoelectric Effect]] (1905) and special relativity were published in the same year — both earned Einstein foundational recognition, with the photoelectric effect winning the Nobel Prize
- Relativistic corrections are essential in [[Particle Identification]] and [[Time-of-Flight]] measurements at accelerator facilities, where particles approach the speed of light
