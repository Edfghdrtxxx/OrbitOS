# Coulomb Excitation

## Definition

Coulomb excitation is a nuclear reaction mechanism in which a projectile nucleus is excited to a higher energy state purely through the long-range electromagnetic (Coulomb) interaction with a target nucleus, without requiring nuclear contact. Because the interaction is exclusively electromagnetic, the excitation cross section can be calculated exactly, making it a model-independent probe of collective nuclear properties.

## Key Points

- **"Safe" Coulomb excitation:** beam energy kept well below the [[Coulomb Barrier]] so that the minimum distance of approach exceeds the nuclear-force range; the process is purely electromagnetic with no nuclear background
- **Relativistic Coulomb excitation:** at intermediate energies ($\sim 100$–$300\;\text{MeV/u}$), the virtual-photon spectrum extends to high energy, enabling excitation of giant resonances; used extensively with [[Radioactive Isotope Beam|radioactive beams]] at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN]] and FRIB
- Directly measures reduced transition probabilities $B(E\lambda)$ (especially $B(E2)$), providing quantitative information on nuclear deformation and collectivity
- A key tool for studying exotic nuclei produced via [[In-Flight Fission]] or [[Projectile Fragmentation]], where traditional spectroscopy methods have limited statistics

## Examples

- **$B(E2)$ measurements of neutron-rich Cr and Fe isotopes at RIKEN:** Coulomb excitation of beams from [[In-Flight Fission]] of ${}^{238}\text{U}$ revealed the onset of deformation near $N = 40$
- **Giant dipole resonance (GDR) studies:** relativistic Coulomb excitation dissociates halo nuclei (e.g., ${}^{11}\text{Be}$), probing their extended neutron distributions
