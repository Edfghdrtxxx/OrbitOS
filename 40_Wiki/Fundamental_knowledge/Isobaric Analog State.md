# Isobaric Analog State

## Definition

An isobaric analog state (IAS) is an excited state in a nucleus $(A, Z+1)$ that mirrors the ground state (or a specific state) of its isobar $(A, Z)$, differing only by the replacement of a neutron with a proton. The two states share the same isospin quantum number $T$ and spatial-spin wave function, separated in energy primarily by the [[Coulomb Barrier|Coulomb]] displacement energy $\Delta E_C$.

## Key Points

- IAS arise from the approximate charge independence of the nuclear force: the strong interaction is nearly symmetric under isospin rotation, so states related by $T_z \to T_z \pm 1$ have similar structure
- The energy difference between an IAS and its parent state is dominated by the Coulomb energy shift, typically $\Delta E_C \approx 1.4\,Z/A^{1/3}$ MeV, providing a precise test of isospin symmetry breaking
- IAS are populated experimentally via [[Charge-Exchange Reaction]]s such as $(p, n)$ or $({}^3\text{He}, t)$, where a neutron in the target converts to a proton (or vice versa)
- The isospin purity of IAS is broken by Coulomb and charge-dependent nuclear forces, leading to small isospin mixing observable as fine structure in the IAS peak
- IAS systematics constrain the neutron skin thickness of neutron-rich nuclei through the Lane potential

## Examples

- The IAS of the $^{48}$Ca ground state ($T = 4$) appears as a narrow resonance in $^{48}$Sc at $\sim 6$ MeV excitation energy, accessible via $^{48}\text{Ca}(p, n)^{48}\text{Sc}$
- In light nuclei, the $T = 1$ states in $^{14}$N are isobaric analogs of the $^{14}$C and $^{14}$O ground states
