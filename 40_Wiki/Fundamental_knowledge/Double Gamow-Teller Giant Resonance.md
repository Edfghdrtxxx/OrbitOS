# Double Gamow-Teller Giant Resonance
## Definition

The Double Gamow-Teller Giant Resonance (DGTGR) is a two-phonon giant resonance formed by two successive applications of the [[Gamow-Teller Transition|Gamow-Teller]] operator, resulting in a double spin-flip, double isospin-flip excitation with $\Delta Z = \pm 2$. It was first predicted theoretically by Auerbach, Zamick & Zheng (1989).

## Transition Operator

$$\hat{O}_{DGT} = \sum_{k,l} (\boldsymbol{\sigma}_k \tau^-_k)(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_l \tau^-_l)$$

where the sum runs over nucleon pairs $(k, l)$. The operator converts two neutrons into two protons (or vice versa), connecting nuclei $(A, Z)$ and $(A, Z \pm 2)$.

## Connection to Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay

The DGTGR has gained major significance because its nuclear matrix element correlates linearly with the matrix element $M^{0\nu}$ governing **neutrinoless double-beta decay** ($0\nu\beta\beta$), as shown by Shimizu et al. (PRL, 2018). Since $M^{0\nu}$ is model-dependent with uncertainties of factors $\sim 2$--$3$, measuring DGT strength experimentally provides an independent constraint crucial for interpreting $0\nu\beta\beta$ half-life measurements and extracting the neutrino mass.

## Experimental Access

The DGTGR is accessed via **heavy-ion double charge-exchange reactions**, e.g.:

$${}^{48}\text{Ca}({}^{12}\text{C}, {}^{12}\text{Be}(0^+_2))$$

at $250$ MeV/u at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN RIBF]]. The ${}^{12}\text{Be}(0^+_2)$ state is selected because it requires two sequential GT-type spin-flip transitions through a ${}^{12}\text{B}(1^+)$ intermediate. This state decays via an $E0$ transition by internal pair creation ($e^+e^-$), producing characteristic 511 keV $\gamma$-rays as an experimental tag.

## First Candidate Observation (2024)

Sasano, Yako, Imai et al. reported the first candidate observation in ${}^{48}\text{Ca}$ (PTEP, 2024): forward-angle cross section of $1.33 \pm 0.12$ $\mu$b/sr below 34 MeV excitation energy, with ~40% attributed to DGT via [[DWBA]] multipole decomposition. The result remains a "candidate" pending higher statistics.
