# Radioactive Decay

## Definition

Radioactive Decay is the spontaneous transformation of an unstable atomic nucleus into a more stable configuration by emitting particles or [[Gamma Ray|electromagnetic radiation]], governed by quantum-mechanical tunnelling and selection rules — it is characterised by the [[Half-Life]] and measured in [[Becquerel|becquerels]].

## Key Points

- **Modes**: Alpha decay (emission of ⁴He nucleus), beta decay (β⁻: neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino; β⁺: proton → neutron + positron + neutrino), gamma emission ([[Nuclear Transition]] between excited states), and less common modes (electron capture, internal conversion, spontaneous fission)
- **Decay law**: $N(t) = N_0 \, e^{-\lambda t}$, where $\lambda = \ln 2 / t_{1/2}$ is the decay constant; activity $A = \lambda N$ is measured in [[Becquerel]] (1 Bq = 1 decay/s)
- **Energy source**: The [[Binding Energy]] difference between parent and daughter nuclei provides the Q-value that is distributed as kinetic energy among decay products
- **Decay chains**: Heavy [[Radioisotope|radioisotopes]] (²³⁸U, ²³²Th) undergo sequential decays through multiple daughters until reaching a stable end product, producing spectra of [[Gamma Ray|gamma rays]] used for isotope identification in [[Gamma Spectroscopy]]

## Examples

- ¹³⁷Cs undergoes β⁻ decay ($t_{1/2}$ = 30.17 yr) to ¹³⁷ᵐBa, which de-excites via a 662 keV [[Gamma Ray]] — a standard calibration source for [[Energy Resolution]] testing
- ²²⁶Ra (α-decay, $t_{1/2}$ = 1600 yr) heads a decay chain producing radon gas, a significant source of natural background radiation monitored under the [[ALARA Principle]]
