# Inverse Kinematics

## Definition

Inverse kinematics is an experimental technique in nuclear physics where a heavier beam is accelerated onto a lighter target (typically hydrogen or deuterium), reversing the conventional arrangement. This kinematic inversion focuses the reaction products into a narrow forward cone, enabling detection and identification of products from reactions on short-lived [[Radioactive Isotope Beam|radioactive isotope beams]] that cannot serve as fixed targets.

## Key Points

- In **normal kinematics**, a light projectile (e.g., proton) hits a heavy target; in **inverse kinematics**, the heavy ion is the beam and the light nucleus is the target
- The Lorentz boost from the massive beam compresses the center-of-mass angular distribution into a small laboratory forward angle, so nearly all reaction products travel downstream and can be collected by a [[Magnetic Spectrometer|magnetic spectrometer]] such as [[SHARAQ Spectrometer|SHARAQ]]
- Essential for studying unstable nuclei: since short-lived isotopes ($t_{1/2} \lesssim 1$ s) cannot be fabricated into targets, they must be used as beams -- making inverse kinematics the only viable approach
- Widely used for [[Transfer Reactions]], [[Knockout Reactions]], [[Charge-Exchange Reaction|charge-exchange reactions]], and [[Surrogate Reaction|surrogate reactions]] at facilities like RIKEN and the [[OEDO]] beamline
- The recoiling light particle (e.g., proton, deuteron) is detected by dedicated arrays (e.g., silicon telescopes using the [[ΔE-E Method]]) to reconstruct the excitation energy of the heavy residue

## Examples

- ${}^{132}\text{Sn}(d,p)$ in inverse kinematics: a ${}^{132}$Sn beam hits a deuterium target to study neutron-transfer reactions relevant to the [[Neutron Capture Cross Section|r-process]]
- ${}^{79}\text{Se}(d,p)$ at [[OEDO]]/RIKEN: [[Surrogate Reaction|surrogate reaction]] measurement to extract $(n,\gamma)$ cross sections for nuclear waste transmutation (Imai et al., 2024)
