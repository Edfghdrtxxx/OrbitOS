# Liquid Drop Model

## Definition

The Liquid Drop Model treats the atomic nucleus as a drop of incompressible nuclear fluid, where nucleons interact via short-range forces analogous to molecules in a liquid. It underpins the semi-empirical mass formula (Bethe–Weizsäcker formula) that estimates [[Binding Energy]] as a sum of volume, surface, Coulomb, asymmetry, and pairing terms.

## Key Points

- Successfully explains the overall trend of $B/A$ across the nuclear chart and the energetics of nuclear fission (Bohr & Wheeler, 1939)
- The five terms: $B = a_V A - a_S A^{2/3} - a_C \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}} - a_A \frac{(N-Z)^2}{4A} + \delta(A,Z)$
- **Critical limitation:** it predicts a smooth $B/A$ curve and cannot reproduce the sudden jumps at [[Nuclear Magic Numbers]] (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126)
- This failure motivated the [[Nuclear Shell Model]], which adds single-particle quantum structure on top of the collective picture

## Examples

- The Liquid Drop Model accurately predicts the fission barrier of $^{235}\text{U}$, explaining why thermal neutrons can induce fission
- It fails to explain why $^{208}\text{Pb}$ is anomalously stable compared to its neighbors — a shell effect
