# Even-Even Nucleus

## Schematics

*No suitable open-license schematics found on Wikimedia Commons for this topic.*

## Definition

An even-even nucleus is a nuclide with an even number of protons ($Z$) and an even number of neutrons ($N$), whose ground state is always $J^\pi = 0^+$ because all nucleons form pairs that couple their [[Angular Momentum|angular momenta]] to zero.

## Key Points

- The universal $0^+$ ground state is a direct consequence of the nuclear pairing interaction: identical nucleons preferentially pair into states with total angular momentum $J = 0$, maximizing [[Binding Energy|binding energy]]
- Even-even nuclei are systematically more bound than their odd-$A$ or odd-odd neighbors, producing the characteristic pairing staggering visible in the [[B_A curve|binding energy per nucleon curve]]
- The first excited state is almost always $2^+$ (a collective quadrupole excitation); the energy $E(2^+_1)$ is a sensitive indicator of [[Shell closure|shell closure]] (high $E(2^+_1)$ near [[Magic Numbers]]) versus collectivity (low $E(2^+_1)$ in deformed regions)
- [[Low-lying 0+ Excited State|Low-lying $0^+$ excited states]] in even-even nuclei signal [[Deformation Coexistence]] — an alternative shape configuration at nearly the same energy as the ground state
- Transitions between $0^+$ states proceed exclusively via [[E0 Transition|E0 (electric monopole) transitions]], providing a unique probe of shape changes

## Examples

- $^{208}$Pb ($Z = 82$, $N = 126$) is the heaviest doubly-magic even-even nucleus, with a very high $E(2^+_1) \approx 4.1$ MeV reflecting strong [[Shell closure|shell closures]]
- $^{186}$Pb exhibits triple [[Deformation Coexistence|shape coexistence]] with three $0^+$ states within 650 keV — a dramatic consequence of competing configurations in an even-even system
