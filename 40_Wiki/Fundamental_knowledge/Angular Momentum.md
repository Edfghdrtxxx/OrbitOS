# Angular Momentum

## Definition

Angular momentum is a conserved vector quantity describing rotational motion. In quantum mechanics, orbital angular momentum $\vec{L}$ and intrinsic spin $\vec{S}$ are quantized, with their coupling $\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}$ governed by [[Quantum Numbers]] $l$, $s$, and $j$. The operators satisfy commutation relations $[L_i, L_j] = i\hbar\,\epsilon_{ijk}\,L_k$.

## Key Points

- Orbital angular momentum has magnitude $|\vec{L}| = \sqrt{l(l+1)}\,\hbar$ with $z$-projection $L_z = m_l\hbar$, where $l = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ and $m_l = -l, \dots, +l$
- Spin is intrinsic angular momentum with no classical analogue; for fermions $s = 1/2$, giving two states $m_s = \pm 1/2$
- Total angular momentum $\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}$ couples via Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, with $j$ ranging from $|l-s|$ to $l+s$
- [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]] ($\vec{L}\cdot\vec{S}$ interaction) splits energy levels by $j$, crucial in both atomic [[Fine Structure]] and the [[Nuclear Shell Model]]
- Angular momentum conservation follows from rotational symmetry (Noether's theorem)

## Examples

- A $p$-orbital electron ($l=1$, $s=1/2$) has $j = 3/2$ or $j = 1/2$, producing the spin-orbit doublet
- The total angular momentum of a nucleus determines its ground-state spin, measurable via nuclear magnetic resonance
