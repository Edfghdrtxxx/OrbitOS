# Spin-Orbit Coupling

## Definition

Spin-orbit coupling is the interaction between a particle's orbital angular momentum $\vec{L}$ and its intrinsic spin angular momentum $\vec{S}$, producing a potential term proportional to $\vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$. In nuclear physics, adding this term to the [[Mean-Field Potential]] splits energy levels by total angular momentum $j = l \pm \frac{1}{2}$, which is essential for reproducing the observed [[Magic Numbers]].

## Key Points

- The spin-orbit potential takes the form $V_{so}(r)\,\vec{L}\cdot\vec{S}$, where the expectation value of $\vec{L}\cdot\vec{S}$ is $\frac{1}{2}[j(j+1) - l(l+1) - s(s+1)]$
- It splits each $(n, l)$ level into two sub-levels: $j = l + \frac{1}{2}$ (lower energy, more degenerate) and $j = l - \frac{1}{2}$ (higher energy) — note the sign is opposite to the atomic case
- This splitting explains the [[Magic Numbers]] (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) that the [[Liquid Drop Model]] and simple [[Harmonic Oscillator|harmonic oscillator]] potentials cannot reproduce
- First proposed by Mayer and Jensen (1949, Nobel Prize 1963) as the key ingredient of the [[Nuclear Shell Model]]
- In atomic physics, the same interaction causes [[Fine Structure|fine structure]] splitting of [[Spectral Lines|spectral lines]], but with opposite sign convention

## Examples

- The $1f$ level splits into $1f_{7/2}$ ($j = 7/2$, 8 states) and $1f_{5/2}$ ($j = 5/2$, 6 states); the large gap above $1f_{7/2}$ creates the magic number 28
- In the hydrogen atom, spin-orbit coupling splits the $2p$ state into $2p_{3/2}$ and $2p_{1/2}$, producing the sodium D-line doublet
