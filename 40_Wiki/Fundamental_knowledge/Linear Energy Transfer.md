# Linear Energy Transfer

## Definition

Linear Energy Transfer (LET) is the amount of energy deposited by [[Ionizing Radiation]] per unit path length in a medium (typically $\text{keV/\mu m}$), and is the key parameter determining the biological effectiveness of different radiation types for [[Dosimetry]] and [[Sievert]] calculations.

## Key Points

- High-LET radiation (alpha particles, heavy ions) deposits energy densely along the track, causing more severe and clustered DNA damage
- Low-LET radiation (gamma rays, electrons) deposits energy sparsely, producing more repairable single-strand breaks
- LET directly determines the [[Radiation Weighting Factor]] ($w_R$) used to convert [[Absorbed Dose]] ($\text{Gy}$) to equivalent dose ([[Sievert|Sv]])
- The [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]] provides the theoretical framework for calculating energy loss (related to LET) of [[Charged Particle]]s in matter
- [[ALARA Principle]] implementation requires LET awareness when working with mixed radiation fields in a [[Radiation Controlled Area]]

## Examples

- Alpha particles have LET $\approx 100$–$200\;\text{keV/\mu m}$ in tissue and carry a [[Radiation Weighting Factor]] of 20, while gamma rays have LET $\approx 0.2\;\text{keV/\mu m}$ with a weighting factor of 1
- In hadron therapy, high-LET carbon ions are used to maximize [[Relative Biological Effectiveness]] in the tumor Bragg peak while sparing surrounding tissue
