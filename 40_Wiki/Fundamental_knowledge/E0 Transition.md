# E0 Transition

## Schematics

*No suitable open-license schematics found on Wikimedia Commons for this topic.*

## Definition

An E0 (electric monopole) transition is an electromagnetic [[Nuclear Transition|nuclear transition]] between two states of identical spin-parity ($J^\pi \to J^\pi$, most importantly $0^+ \to 0^+$) that cannot proceed by photon emission and instead occurs exclusively via internal conversion or internal pair creation.

## Key Points

- A real photon must carry at least one unit of [[Angular Momentum|angular momentum]] ($L \geq 1$), so $0^+ \to 0^+$ transitions are strictly forbidden for single-photon emission — E0 is the only available electromagnetic channel
- The E0 transition operator is proportional to $\sum_i e_i r_i^2$, making the monopole strength $\rho^2(E0)$ directly sensitive to changes in the nuclear mean-square charge radius $\langle r^2 \rangle$ between initial and final states
- Large $\rho^2(E0)$ values between $0^+$ states are the most direct experimental signature of [[Deformation Coexistence]] — they indicate that the two states have substantially different nuclear shapes
- E0 transitions are detected experimentally through internal conversion electrons (measured with magnetic spectrometers or Si(Li) detectors) or $e^+e^-$ pairs for high-energy transitions
- The E0 strength is not just a $0^+ \to 0^+$ phenomenon — in $J \to J$ transitions with $J > 0$, E0 contributions can accompany higher-multipole radiation (E2, M1), and must be separated experimentally

## Examples

- In $^{186}$Pb, the large $\rho^2(E0)$ values between the three [[Low-lying 0+ Excited State|low-lying $0^+$ states]] confirm that each state corresponds to a different nuclear shape (spherical, oblate, prolate)
- In the neutron-rich Sr and Zr isotopes (e.g., $^{96}$Sr), large $\rho^2(E0)$ values between coexisting $0^+$ states track the sudden onset of deformation at $N = 60$
