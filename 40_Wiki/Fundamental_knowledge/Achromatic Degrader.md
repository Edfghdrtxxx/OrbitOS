# Achromatic Degrader

## Definition

An achromatic degrader is a shaped energy-loss material (typically an aluminium or beryllium wedge) placed at the dispersive focal plane of a fragment separator. Its thickness varies with horizontal position to compensate for the momentum spread of the beam, so that downstream optics remain achromatic — i.e., particles of different momenta converge back to the same trajectory after the degrader.

## Key Points

- Sits between the two [[Magnetic Rigidity|Bρ]]-selection stages of a separator, enabling the Bρ–ΔE–Bρ method: first Bρ selects $A/Z$, the degrader introduces Z-dependent energy loss via the [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]], and the second Bρ separates isotopes that originally had the same $A/Z$
- The wedge profile is machined so that thicker material intercepts higher-rigidity fragments at larger dispersion offsets, equalising the outgoing $B\rho$ spread — this preserves the beam's phase-space quality (emittance)
- Without the achromatic shape, momentum spread after the degrader would blow up, drastically reducing transmission and [[Isotope Separation]] purity
- Material choice balances atomic number (higher $Z$ → more [[dE-dx|$dE/dx$]] per unit thickness) against nuclear reactions that destroy the fragments of interest

## Examples

- [[BigRIPS]] uses an Al wedge degrader at its F1 dispersive focus; the degrader thickness profile is optimised for each experiment's target isotope
- The A1900 separator at MSU/NSCL and the FRS at GSI employ analogous achromatic degraders in their Bρ–ΔE–Bρ purification schemes
