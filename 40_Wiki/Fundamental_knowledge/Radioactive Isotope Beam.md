# Radioactive Isotope Beam

## Definition

A Radioactive Isotope Beam (RIB) is a beam of unstable (radioactive) nuclei produced at accelerator facilities, used to study nuclear structure, reactions, and astrophysical processes far from the valley of stability.

## Key Points

- **Production**: Typically produced by fragmenting a high-energy stable beam on a production target, then selecting the desired isotope using magnetic separators (e.g., [[BigRIPS]], RIBLL at [[Institute of Modern Physics|IMP]])
- **"Exotic" RIB**: Refers to isotopes far from the valley of stability (very neutron-rich or proton-rich); the farther from stability, the lower the production rate — ranging from $10^0$ to $10^3$ pps for the most exotic species
- **Intensity spectrum**: Stable/primary beams reach $10^6$–$10^9$ pps, moderately exotic RIBs $10^3$–$10^6$ pps, and the most exotic species may yield only a few particles per second
- **Identification**: Each particle must be identified event-by-event using [[Time-of-Flight]], [[Bethe-Bloch Formula|$\Delta E$]], and [[Magnetic Rigidity|$B\rho$]] measurements due to the cocktail of isotopes produced simultaneously

## Examples

- At RIKEN's [[BigRIPS]], a $^{238}$U beam is fragmented to produce neutron-rich nuclei near the neutron drip line for shell-structure studies
- At IMP's HIRFL-RIBLL, RIBs are used for direct reaction experiments with [[Knockout Reactions|knockout]] and transfer reactions on inverse-kinematics targets
