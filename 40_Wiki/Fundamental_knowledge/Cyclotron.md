# Cyclotron

## Definition

A cyclotron is a circular particle accelerator that uses a constant, uniform magnetic field and a fixed-frequency RF electric field to accelerate charged particles in a spiral path outward from the centre. The cyclotron frequency $f_c = qB/(2\pi m)$ is independent of radius (non-relativistically), so a single RF frequency can repeatedly accelerate the particles each half-turn.

## Key Points

- Invented by Ernest Lawrence (1932); the simplest circular accelerator — two hollow D-shaped electrodes ("dees") sit inside a uniform dipole magnet
- The resonance condition $f_\text{RF} = f_c = qB/(2\pi m)$ holds only for non-relativistic particles; as $v \to c$ the relativistic mass increase $\gamma m$ causes the orbit frequency to drop, breaking resonance
- **Isochronous cyclotron** fix: radially increasing $B(r)$ compensates the relativistic mass rise so that $f_c$ stays constant — used in modern facilities like HIRFL at [[Institute of Modern Physics]] and RIBF at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN]]
- Maximum kinetic energy is limited by the magnet pole radius $R$ and field $B$: $T_\text{max} \approx q^2 B^2 R^2 / (2m)$ (non-relativistic limit)
- Cyclotrons are workhorses for producing radioactive ion beams (RIBs) via projectile fragmentation and for medical isotope production (e.g., ${}^{18}\text{F}$ for PET scans)

## Examples

- **HIRFL (IMP):** The sector-focused cyclotron complex at [[Institute of Modern Physics|IMP]] accelerates heavy ions up to uranium for nuclear physics experiments
- **Medical cyclotrons:** Compact cyclotrons ($\sim 1\;\text{m}$ radius) produce proton beams of $\sim 10$–$30\;\text{MeV}$ for PET radioisotope synthesis
