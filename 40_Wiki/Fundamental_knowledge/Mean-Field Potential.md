# Mean-Field Potential

## Definition

A mean-field potential is an average potential felt by a single nucleon due to the combined interactions of all other nucleons in the nucleus. Instead of solving the intractable many-body problem exactly, each nucleon is treated as an independent particle moving in this smoothed-out potential — the **independent particle approximation** at the heart of the [[Nuclear Shell Model]].

## Key Points

- Common choices: harmonic oscillator (analytically solvable, good first approximation), Woods-Saxon potential (more realistic — flat interior with a diffuse surface edge)
- A pure mean-field potential predicts shell closures at 2, 8, 20 only; adding [[Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei|spin-orbit coupling]] splits levels and reproduces all [[Nuclear Magic Numbers]]
- Modern approaches derive the mean field self-consistently from nucleon-nucleon interactions using Hartree-Fock theory
- The mean-field picture breaks down for highly deformed or weakly-bound nuclei, where collective and continuum effects dominate

## Examples

- The Woods-Saxon potential $V(r) = -\frac{V_0}{1 + e^{(r-R)/a}}$ mimics the nuclear density distribution — constant inside, falling off at the surface
- Ab initio calculations now derive effective mean fields from chiral effective field theory (NN + 3N forces rooted in QCD)
