# Projectile Fragmentation

## Definition

Projectile fragmentation is a nuclear reaction in which a high-energy heavy-ion projectile undergoes peripheral collisions with a light target (e.g., ${}^{9}\text{Be}$, ${}^{12}\text{C}$), causing removal of nucleons primarily from the projectile surface. The surviving prefragment de-excites by particle emission, producing a range of exotic nuclei that are separated in-flight by a magnetic [[Magnetic Rigidity|fragment separator]].

## Key Points

- **Peripheral mechanism:** only the overlap region of projectile and target interacts; the projectile "core" survives largely intact, so fragments retain beam-like velocity and forward focusing
- **Proton-rich competitiveness:** produces both proton-rich and neutron-rich isotopes, but is *particularly competitive* on the proton-rich side relative to [[In-Flight Fission]], which excels at neutron-rich production around $A \sim 80$–$160$
- Fragments are identified event-by-event via [[Magnetic Rigidity|$B\rho$]]–$\Delta E$–TOF using the [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]] for energy-loss determination
- In-flight separation is fast ($\sim\mu\text{s}$), enabling study of nuclei with very short [[Half-Life|half-lives]] — far faster than [[ISOL]] methods
- Key facilities: [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN]] ([[BigRIPS]]), FRIB (A1900 → ARIS), GSI (FRS)

## Examples

- **Discovery of ${}^{100}\text{Sn}$:** the doubly-magic proton-rich nucleus was first observed nearly simultaneously at GSI (Schneider et al.) and GANIL (Lewitowicz et al.) in 1994, both via projectile fragmentation
- **Knockout reactions at RIKEN:** single-nucleon removal from fragmentation-produced beams reveals shell structure of exotic nuclei far from stability
