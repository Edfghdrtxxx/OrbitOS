# Subshell Closure at Z = 6

## Definition

The subshell closure at proton number Z = 6 is the smallest magic number originating from [[Spin-Orbit Coupling in Nuclei|spin-orbit coupling]]. It corresponds to the complete filling of the 1p₃/₂ proton subshell in carbon isotopes, producing enhanced stability in neutron-rich carbon nuclei (¹³⁻²⁰C).

## Key Points

- Experimentally established by Tran et al. (2018) through systematic analysis of three independent observables in neutron-rich carbon isotopes:
  1. **Point-proton distribution radii** — measured via interaction cross sections at RIBF (RIKEN); carbon radii are systematically smaller than neighboring B and N isotopes, indicating a tightly bound proton core
  2. **B(E2) transition rates** — low E2 transition probabilities in even-even carbon isotopes (¹⁶C, ¹⁸C, ²⁰C) signal a rigid, closed proton configuration
  3. **Proton separation energies (Sp)** — large Sp values for carbon relative to neighbors confirm extra proton binding at Z = 6
- The closure persists across the entire range of neutron-rich carbon isotopes from ¹³C to ²⁰C, demonstrating it is **prevalent** (robust against adding neutrons)
- Ab initio coupled-cluster calculations with chiral NN+3N forces reproduce the observed proton radii and the subshell closure, validating the role of three-nucleon forces

## Significance

- Smallest spin-orbit-originated magic number — a direct window into the strength and nature of the nuclear SO force
- Demonstrates that modern nuclear theory (chiral EFT) can predict shell structure from QCD-rooted interactions
- Extends the set of known [[Nuclear Magic Numbers]] to include local subshell effects in light nuclei

## Experimental Method

Interaction cross sections of ¹³⁻²⁰C on a carbon target were measured at ~900 MeV/nucleon using the BigRIPS fragment separator at RIBF (RIKEN). Point-proton radii were extracted using a Glauber model analysis combined with the proton and neutron density distributions.
