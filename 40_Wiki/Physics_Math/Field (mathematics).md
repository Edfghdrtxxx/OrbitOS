# Field (mathematics)

## Definition

A **field** is a set $F$ equipped with two operations — addition and multiplication — satisfying axioms of [[Commutativity]], [[Associativity]], [[Distributivity]], and the existence of [[Identity Element|identity]] and [[Inverse Element|inverse]] elements for both operations. Common examples: $\mathbb{R}$ (real numbers), $\mathbb{C}$ (complex numbers), $\mathbb{Q}$ (rationals).

## Key Points

- A [[Vector_Spaces|vector space]] is defined **over** a field — the field provides the scalars used in [[Linear Combination|linear combinations]]
- $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$ are the most common fields in physics; quantum mechanics uses $\mathbb{C}$
- Every field contains exactly two distinguished elements: the [[Identity Element|additive identity]] $0$ and the [[Identity Element|multiplicative identity]] $1$
- [[Finite Field|Finite fields]] (e.g., $\mathbb{F}_p$ for prime $p$) are important in coding theory and cryptography

## Examples

- $\mathbb{R}$: real numbers with standard $+$ and $\times$ — the default field for classical physics
- $\mathbb{C}$: complex numbers — required for quantum mechanics because state amplitudes are complex
- $\mathbb{Q}$: rational numbers — a [[Subfield]] of $\mathbb{R}$
- $\mathbb{Z}$ (integers) is **not** a field because most elements lack a multiplicative [[Inverse Element|inverse]] (e.g., $2$ has no integer $2^{-1}$)
