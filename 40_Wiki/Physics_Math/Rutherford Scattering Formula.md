# Rutherford Scattering Formula

## Definition

The Rutherford scattering formula gives the [[Differential Cross Section]] for the elastic Coulomb scattering of a point charged projectile off a point target nucleus, assuming a pure $1/r$ Coulomb potential with no nuclear interaction.

## Key Points

- The formula in the center-of-mass frame is:
$$\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left(\frac{a}{4}\right)^2 \frac{1}{\sin^4(\theta/2)}, \quad a = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 \cdot 2E_{\text{cm}}}$$
  where $a$ is half the distance of closest approach, $Z_1, Z_2$ are the projectile and target atomic numbers, and $E_{\text{cm}}$ is the center-of-mass kinetic energy.
- The $1/\sin^4(\theta/2)$ dependence means the cross section diverges at forward angles ($\theta \to 0$) — most particles scatter at small angles, and large-angle scattering is rare.
- Deviations from the Rutherford prediction at higher energies or smaller impact parameters signal the onset of nuclear forces, and the ratio $\sigma_{\text{exp}} / \sigma_{\text{Ruth}}$ is used to extract nuclear [[Differential Cross Section|cross sections]] in [[Direct Reactions]] and [[Transfer Reactions]].
- Historically derived by Rutherford (1911) to explain the Geiger-Marsden gold foil experiment, providing the first evidence for a compact atomic nucleus.

## Examples

- In elastic scattering experiments, the measured angular distribution is compared to the Rutherford formula to normalize absolute cross sections and identify the [[Coulomb Barrier|Coulomb-nuclear interference]] region.
- At energies well below the [[Coulomb Barrier]], the scattering is purely Rutherford and is used for detector calibration and beam normalization.
