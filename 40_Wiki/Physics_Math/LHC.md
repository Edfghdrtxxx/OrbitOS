## Definition

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world's largest and highest-energy particle accelerator, operated by [[CERN]] near Geneva, Switzerland. It is a circular synchrotron with a circumference of $26.659\;\text{km}$, installed in the tunnel formerly occupied by the Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP) at a depth of approximately $100\;\text{m}$ beneath the Franco-Swiss border. The LHC accelerates counter-rotating beams of protons (or heavy ions such as ${}^{208}\text{Pb}$) and brings them into collision at four interaction points, reaching a centre-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s} = 13.6\;\text{TeV}$ for proton-proton collisions in Run 3 (2022--present).

## Key Points

- **Injector chain**: Protons are produced from hydrogen gas, pre-accelerated through LINAC4 ($160\;\text{MeV}$), the Proton Synchrotron Booster ($2\;\text{GeV}$), the Proton Synchrotron (PS, $26\;\text{GeV}$), and the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS, $450\;\text{GeV}$) before injection into the LHC
- **Superconducting magnets**: The two counter-circulating beams are guided by 1,232 dipole magnets producing a field of $8.33\;\text{T}$, cooled to $1.9\;\text{K}$ using superfluid helium — colder than outer space
- **Design luminosity**: Peak instantaneous luminosity of $\mathcal{L} = 1 \times 10^{34}\;\text{cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}$ (exceeded in Run 2), enabling rare process searches through high event rates
- **Four main experiments**:
  - **ATLAS** — general-purpose detector; co-discovered the [[Higgs Boson]] (2012)
  - **CMS** — general-purpose detector; independent Higgs boson co-discovery
  - **ALICE** — dedicated to heavy-ion collisions, studying [[Quark-Gluon Plasma]]
  - **LHCb** — specialised in $b$-quark physics, [[CP Violation]], and flavour physics
- **Key milestone**: On 4 July 2012, ATLAS and CMS announced the observation of a new boson at $\sim125\;\text{GeV}$, consistent with the [[Higgs Boson]] predicted by the [[Standard Model]] — earning the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physics for François Englert and Peter Higgs
- **Beam structure**: Bunches of $\sim1.15 \times 10^{11}$ protons separated by $25\;\text{ns}$, with up to 2,556 bunches per beam

## Examples

- The LHC's heavy-ion programme collides lead nuclei at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.36\;\text{TeV}$ per nucleon pair, creating conditions that existed microseconds after the Big Bang, studied primarily by the ALICE experiment using a large [[Time Projection Chamber]]
- The High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), planned for operation from $\sim$2029, will upgrade the peak luminosity by a factor of $5$--$7.5$ beyond the design value, enabling precision measurements of Higgs boson couplings and searches for rare decays
