# Coulomb Barrier

## Definition

The Coulomb barrier is the electrostatic potential energy that two positively charged nuclei must overcome to approach closely enough for the short-range nuclear force to act, calculated as the maximum of the combined Coulomb-plus-nuclear potential.

## Key Points

- The barrier height for two nuclei with atomic numbers $Z_1, Z_2$ and radii $R_1, R_2$ is approximately:
$$V_C = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 (R_1 + R_2)}$$
  Using $R \approx 1.2\,A^{1/3}$ fm, this gives a practical estimate in MeV.
- At bombarding energies well below $V_C$, only [[Rutherford Scattering Formula|Rutherford (Coulomb) scattering]] occurs; nuclear reactions are exponentially suppressed by the tunneling probability.
- At energies near or above $V_C$, nuclear absorption sets in and the elastic scattering angular distribution deviates from the Rutherford prediction — the transition region is called the Coulomb-nuclear interference region.
- Quantum tunneling through the barrier is essential for sub-barrier fusion (e.g., stellar nucleosynthesis) and is described by the Gamow factor $e^{-2\pi\eta}$, where $\eta$ is the Sommerfeld parameter.

## Examples

- For $\alpha + {}^{197}\text{Au}$: $V_C \approx \frac{2 \times 79 \times 1.44}{1.2(4^{1/3}+197^{1/3})} \approx 24\,\text{MeV}$.
- In [[Direct Reactions]] and [[Transfer Reactions]], the beam energy is chosen relative to $V_C$ to control whether the reaction probes the nuclear surface or interior.
