# Silicon Detector

## Definition

A silicon detector is a semiconductor-based radiation detector that measures the energy deposited by charged particles passing through a silicon wafer. Incident radiation creates electron-hole pairs in the depleted region; the collected charge is proportional to the deposited energy, enabling precise $\Delta E$ or total $E$ measurements.

## Key Points

- Excellent [[Energy Resolution|energy resolution]] (~0.1–1%) due to the small energy required to create an electron-hole pair in silicon ($\sim 3.6\,\text{eV}$)
- Available in various configurations: single-pad, strip (position-sensitive), and pixelated (e.g., DSSD — Double-Sided Silicon Strip Detector)
- Often used in telescope arrangements for [[PID|particle identification]] via the [[ΔE-E Method]] — a thin $\Delta E$ layer followed by a thick $E$ stopping layer
- Sensitive to radiation damage at high fluences, which degrades resolution over time

## Examples

- In the [[RCNP]] ATPC experiment, silicon detectors downstream of the [[Time Projection Chamber|TPC]] provide $\Delta E$–$E$ information for [[PID]], with strip readout giving position sensitivity
- [[BigRIPS]] uses silicon detectors at focal planes for energy-loss measurements complementing [[PPAC]] position and [[Scintillation Detector|plastic scintillator]] TOF data
