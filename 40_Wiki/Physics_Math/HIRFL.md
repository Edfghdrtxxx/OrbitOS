# HIRFL

## Definition

HIRFL (Heavy Ion Research Facility in Lanzhou) is the main heavy-ion accelerator complex at the [[Institute of Modern Physics]] (IMP), Chinese Academy of Sciences, in Lanzhou, China. It consists of a two-[[Cyclotron]] injector system coupled to the [[CSR]] cooler-storage-ring synchrotron, capable of accelerating all ions from proton ($Z=1$) to uranium ($Z=92$).

## Key Points

- **Two-cyclotron chain:** The **Sector-Focusing Cyclotron (SFC)** ($K = 69$, isochronous) pre-accelerates ions to 0.5--17 MeV/u, feeding the **Separated-Sector Cyclotron (SSC)** ($K = 450$), which boosts the beam to up to ~110 MeV/u for light ions or 30--50 MeV/u for medium-mass species. Completed in December 1988, this was the first large-scale heavy-ion accelerator designed and built entirely in China (Xia et al., 2002).
- **Synchrotron extension:** The [[CSR]] complex (CSRm + CSRe) receives beam from the SSC and accelerates it further -- up to ~1 GeV/u for light ions or ~500 MeV/u for uranium -- enabling high-energy [[Projectile Fragmentation]] and precision mass measurements.
- **Ion sources:** Injected by [[Superconducting Electron Cyclotron Resonance]] sources (SECRAL and SECRAL-II), delivering intense beams of highly charged heavy ions across the full periodic table.
- **Radioactive beam production:** Two [[Fragment Separator]] beam lines -- [[RIBLL|RIBLL1]] (at SFC-SSC) and [[RIBLL|RIBLL2]] (at CSR) -- produce [[Radioactive Isotope Beam]]s via in-flight [[Projectile Fragmentation]] for nuclear structure and reaction studies.
- **Experimental areas:** Include the External Target Facility (ETF) for fixed-target reactions, biomedical terminals for heavy-ion therapy research, and the CSRe storage ring for isochronous and Schottky mass spectrometry.

## Examples

- A $^{12}\text{C}^{6+}$ beam accelerated through SFC $\to$ SSC $\to$ CSRm reaches ~400 MeV/u, then fragments on a $^{9}$Be target to produce neutron-rich [[Secondary Beam]]s separated by [[RIBLL|RIBLL2]]
- SECRAL-II delivers record-breaking $^{238}\text{U}^{33+}$ beams at 202 e$\mu$A for the production of the most neutron-rich isotopes accessible at HIRFL
