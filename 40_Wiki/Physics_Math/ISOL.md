# ISOL

## Definition

ISOL (Isotope Separation On-Line) is a method for producing [[Radioactive Isotope Beam|radioactive ion beams]] in which reaction products are created in a thick, hot target, thermalized, chemically or physically extracted into an ion source, mass-separated by an electromagnetic separator, and optionally post-accelerated to the desired energy. The technique yields high-purity, low-emittance beams but is limited by the diffusion and effusion timescale (typically milliseconds to seconds).

## Key Points

- **Thick-target + stop approach:** products are fully stopped, then re-ionised — beam quality (emittance, energy spread) is excellent after mass separation, unlike in-flight methods
- **Chemistry-dependent:** extraction efficiency depends on the element's volatility and chemical reactivity with the target material; refractory elements (e.g., W, Ta) are difficult to extract
- **Timescale limitation:** the diffusion-effusion chain takes $\sim 10\;\text{ms}$–$\text{s}$, excluding access to very short-lived isotopes ($t_{1/2} \lesssim 1\;\text{ms}$) that [[In-Flight Fission]] and [[Projectile Fragmentation]] can reach
- **Complementary to in-flight:** ISOL provides higher-quality beams for precision experiments (traps, collinear laser spectroscopy), while in-flight methods provide fast, universal access
- **Key facilities:** ISOLDE ([[CERN]]), ISAC (TRIUMF), SPIRAL2 (GANIL), HRIBF (ORNL, now decommissioned)

## Examples

- **ISOLDE at CERN:** a 1.4 GeV proton beam strikes a thick UC$_x$ target; radioactive isotopes diffuse out, are ionised, mass-separated, and delivered to experiments — has produced beams of over 1000 isotopes of 70+ elements
- **Collinear laser spectroscopy of exotic Ca isotopes:** ISOL-produced beams at ISOLDE enabled precision charge-radius measurements revealing unexpected shell effects at $N = 32$
