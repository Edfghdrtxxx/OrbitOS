# Lorentz Force

## Definition

The Lorentz force is the total electromagnetic force on a charged particle moving through electric and magnetic fields, given by $\mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$, where $q$ is the charge, $\mathbf{E}$ the electric field, $\mathbf{v}$ the velocity, and $\mathbf{B}$ the magnetic field.

## Key Points

- The magnetic component $q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}$ is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it changes direction but does no work — a charged particle in a uniform $\mathbf{B}$ follows a circular orbit with radius $r = mv/(qB)$, the basis of [[Magnetic Rigidity]]
- The electric component $q\mathbf{E}$ accelerates particles along the field direction — this is how [[RF Electric Field|RF cavities]] and [[Cyclotron]] dees inject energy each half-turn
- Underpins the operating principle of every circular accelerator ([[Cyclotron]], [[Synchrotron]]) and every [[Magnetic Spectrometer]]

## Examples

- **Cyclotron orbit:** A proton in a $1\;\text{T}$ field at $v \ll c$ orbits at the cyclotron frequency $f_c = qB/(2\pi m) \approx 15.2\;\text{MHz}$
- **Beam bending:** Dipole magnets in a [[Synchrotron]] use $q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$ to bend the beam into a closed orbit
