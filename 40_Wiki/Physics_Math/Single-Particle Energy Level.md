# Single-Particle Energy Level

## Definition

A single-particle energy level is the quantized energy eigenstate of one nucleon (proton or neutron) moving independently in the [[Mean-Field Potential|mean-field potential]] created collectively by all other nucleons in the nucleus. The pattern of these levels — and especially the gaps between them — determines [[Shell closure|shell closures]] and [[Magic Numbers|magic numbers]].

## Key Points

- In the [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model|nuclear shell model]], each nucleon occupies an orbital labeled by quantum numbers $(n, l, j)$, where $j = l \pm \frac{1}{2}$ from [[Spin-Orbit Coupling|spin-orbit coupling]]; each orbital holds up to $2j + 1$ nucleons
- The ordering of single-particle levels depends on the chosen potential: a [[Harmonic Oscillator|harmonic oscillator]] gives evenly spaced shells, while a realistic [[Woods-Saxon Potential|Woods-Saxon potential]] plus a strong spin-orbit term reproduces the empirical [[Magic Numbers|magic numbers]] (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126)
- These levels are **not universal constants** — they shift systematically with the neutron-to-proton ratio due to the residual nucleon-nucleon interaction (especially the tensor force from [[Pi Meson|$\pi$]] and [[Rho Meson|$\rho$]] exchange), a phenomenon known as [[Shell Evolution|shell evolution]]
- Experimentally probed via [[Knockout Reactions|knockout reactions]] and [[Transfer Reactions|transfer reactions]], which measure spectroscopic factors and orbital occupancies of individual levels

## Examples

- The large gap above the $1f_{7/2}$ level at nucleon number 28 is what makes $^{48}$Ca ($Z = 20$, $N = 28$) doubly magic — both proton and neutron single-particle level sequences have big gaps at their respective Fermi surfaces
- In oxygen isotopes, the neutron $d_{3/2}$ single-particle level rises as protons are removed from $d_{5/2}$, opening a new gap at $N = 16$ and making $^{24}$O doubly magic — a direct consequence of [[Shell Evolution|shell evolution]]
