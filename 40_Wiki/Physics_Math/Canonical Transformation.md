# Canonical Transformation

## Schematics

*No suitable open-license schematics found on Wikimedia Commons for this topic.*

## Definition

A canonical transformation is a change of coordinates $(q, p) \to (Q, P)$ in [[Phase Space]] that preserves the form of [[Hamiltonian Mechanics|Hamilton's equations of motion]]. Equivalently, it is a transformation that preserves the [[Symplectic Structure]] of phase space — i.e., the [[Poisson Bracket|Poisson brackets]] satisfy $\{Q_i, P_j\} = \delta_{ij}$.

## Key Points

- Canonical transformations are generated by four types of **generating functions** $F_1(q,Q)$, $F_2(q,P)$, $F_3(p,Q)$, $F_4(p,P)$, each relating old and new canonical variables through partial derivatives.
- A time-independent canonical transformation maps one Hamiltonian $H(q,p)$ to a new Kamiltonian $K(Q,P)$; the [[Hamilton-Jacobi Equation]] arises from seeking a canonical transformation that makes $K = 0$ (or a constant), rendering the new variables trivially solvable.
- Infinitesimal canonical transformations are generated by arbitrary phase-space functions $G(q,p)$ via $\delta q_i = \epsilon \frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}$, $\delta p_i = -\epsilon \frac{\partial G}{\partial q_i}$. This connects symmetries to conserved quantities (cf. [[Angular Momentum]] as the generator of rotations).
- In quantum mechanics, canonical transformations correspond to **unitary transformations** in [[Hilbert Space]], preserving the commutation relations $[\hat{Q}_i, \hat{P}_j] = i\hbar \delta_{ij}$.

## Examples

- **Point transformation:** $Q = Q(q)$ is a special canonical transformation where only coordinates (not momenta) are remapped. The [[Harmonic Oscillator]] in Cartesian vs. polar coordinates is a classic case.
- **Exchange transformation:** $F_1 = q Q$ gives $Q = p$, $P = -q$ — swapping coordinates and momenta (with a sign flip), illustrating the symmetric treatment of $q$ and $p$ in Hamiltonian mechanics.
- **Action-angle variables:** For periodic systems, a canonical transformation to action $J$ and angle $\theta$ variables simplifies the motion to $\dot{J} = 0$, $\dot{\theta} = \omega(J)$ — fundamental in perturbation theory and celestial mechanics.
