# Superconducting Electron Cyclotron Resonance

## Definition

Superconducting Electron Cyclotron Resonance (SC-ECR) is a type of [[Ion Source]] that uses superconducting magnets and microwave-driven [[Electron Cyclotron Resonance]] to produce intense beams of highly charged heavy ions for particle accelerators. Electrons in a magnetized plasma absorb microwave energy when the microwave frequency matches their cyclotron frequency ($\omega_{RF} = \omega_c = \frac{eB}{m_e}$), gaining enough energy to strip orbital electrons from gas atoms through successive ionization.

## Key Points

- The resonance condition requires the magnetic field $B$ to satisfy $B = \frac{m_e \omega_{RF}}{e}$; for a typical 28 GHz microwave source, $B \approx 1.0\,\text{T}$
- Superconducting solenoid and sextupole magnets create a minimum-B configuration that confines the hot electron plasma, achieving stronger fields and higher charge states than normal-conducting ECR sources
- Higher microwave frequencies and stronger magnetic confinement (the "frequency scaling law") yield higher charge states and beam intensities — driving the push from 18 GHz to 28 GHz and 45 GHz designs
- Key facilities: SECRAL and SECRAL-II at [[Institute of Modern Physics]] (IMP/CAS), VENUS at LBNL, SC-ECR sources at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)]]

## Examples

- SECRAL-II at IMP delivers record-intensity beams of highly charged ions (e.g., $^{209}\text{Bi}^{31+}$) for the [[HIRFL]] and HIAF accelerator complexes
- The 28 GHz SC-ECR source at RIKEN provides heavy-ion beams for the Radioactive Isotope Beam Factory (RIBF), enabling superheavy element synthesis experiments
