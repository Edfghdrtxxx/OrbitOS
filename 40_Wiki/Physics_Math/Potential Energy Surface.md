# Potential Energy Surface

## Schematics

*No suitable open-license schematics found on Wikimedia Commons for this topic.*

## Definition

A potential energy surface (PES) in nuclear physics is the total energy of a nucleus mapped as a function of deformation parameters (typically the quadrupole deformation $\beta_2$ and triaxiality $\gamma$), revealing the equilibrium shapes as energy minima and the barriers separating coexisting configurations.

## Key Points

- The PES is calculated using self-consistent [[Mean-Field Potential|mean-field]] methods (Hartree-Fock, Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov) or macroscopic-microscopic approaches that combine [[Liquid Drop Model|liquid drop]] energy with [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model|shell]] corrections
- Minima on the PES correspond to stable or metastable nuclear shapes — a single deep minimum indicates a well-defined ground-state shape, while multiple minima at similar energies signal [[Deformation Coexistence|shape coexistence]]
- The $(\beta_2, \gamma)$ landscape distinguishes spherical ($\beta_2 \approx 0$), prolate ($\gamma = 0°$), oblate ($\gamma = 60°$), and triaxial ($0° < \gamma < 60°$) shapes
- Barrier heights between minima govern the degree of shape mixing: low barriers yield strongly mixed states, while high barriers preserve distinct configurations observable as separate [[Low-lying 0+ Excited State|$0^+$ states]]
- [[Intruder States]] create additional minima on the PES when multi-particle multi-hole excitations across a [[Shell closure|shell gap]] generate a deformed configuration competitive in energy with the spherical one

## Examples

- The PES of $^{186}$Pb shows three distinct minima (spherical, oblate, prolate) at nearly the same energy, explaining the observed triple shape coexistence
- In $^{100}$Zr, the PES transitions from a single spherical minimum (at $N = 58$) to a deep prolate minimum (at $N = 60$), corresponding to the sudden ground-state shape change
