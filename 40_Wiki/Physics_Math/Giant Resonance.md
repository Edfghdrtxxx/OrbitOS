# Giant Resonance

## Definition

A giant resonance (GR) is a broad, high-lying collective nuclear excitation in which a large fraction of nucleons oscillate coherently, exhausting a significant portion of an energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR). Giant resonances are classified by their multipolarity $L$, spin transfer $\Delta S$, and isospin transfer $\Delta T$.

## Key Points

- The **Isovector Giant Dipole Resonance** (IVGDR, $L = 1$, $\Delta T = 1$) corresponds to protons and neutrons oscillating out of phase; its centroid energy follows $E_{\text{GDR}} \approx 78\,A^{-1/3}$ MeV and it exhausts nearly 100% of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule
- The **Isoscalar Giant Monopole Resonance** (ISGMR, $L = 0$, $\Delta T = 0$) is a radial "breathing mode" compression oscillation whose energy directly constrains the nuclear incompressibility $K_\infty$, a key parameter of the nuclear equation of state
- The **Isoscalar Giant Quadrupole Resonance** (ISGQR, $L = 2$, $\Delta T = 0$) involves surface shape oscillations and was the first GR observed via inelastic scattering
- Giant resonances are typically observed at excitation energies of $10$--$30$ MeV with widths of several MeV, arising from Landau damping, spreading width, and escape width
- The [[Double Gamow-Teller Giant Resonance]] extends the concept to two-phonon spin-isospin excitations

## Examples

- The GDR in $^{208}$Pb peaks at $\sim 13.5$ MeV and is one of the most precisely measured giant resonances, serving as a benchmark for nuclear models
- ISGMR measurements in Sn and Cd isotopes via $(\alpha, \alpha')$ scattering have been central to determining the nuclear incompressibility
