## Definition

The Fermi surface is the surface in momentum space (or $k$-space) that separates occupied from unoccupied fermion states at absolute zero temperature ($T = 0$). It exists at the [[Fermi energy]] $E_F$ and is defined by the condition $E(\mathbf{k}) = E_F$. In a free electron gas the Fermi surface is a perfect sphere, but in real crystals it is distorted by the periodic lattice potential and [[Brillouin Zone]] boundaries.

## Key Points

- **Shape encodes physics:** The geometry of the Fermi surface is determined by the material's electronic band structure. Measurements via the de Haas-van Alphen effect or angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) reveal its shape directly.
- **Transport properties:** Electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and magnetic susceptibility all depend on the [[Density of States]] at the Fermi surface and the topology of available scattering states.
- **Nuclear physics analogue:** In the [[Nuclear Shell Model]], nucleons fill [[Single-Particle Energy Level|single-particle energy levels]] in the [[Mean-Field Potential]] up to the [[Fermi energy]]. The resulting Fermi surface in nuclear momentum space governs low-energy excitations, pairing correlations ([[BCS Theory]]), and reaction cross sections near closed shells ([[Magic Numbers]]).
- **Nesting and instabilities:** When large parallel sections of the Fermi surface can be connected by a single wavevector $\mathbf{q}$ (Fermi surface nesting), the system is susceptible to charge density waves, spin density waves, or superconducting instabilities.

## Examples

- **Copper:** Nearly spherical Fermi surface with "necks" touching the [[Brillouin Zone]] boundary along the $\langle 111 \rangle$ directions, explaining its high electrical conductivity.
- **Nuclear matter:** In symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density, the proton and neutron Fermi surfaces are concentric spheres at $k_F \approx 1.36\;\text{fm}^{-1}$, corresponding to $E_F \approx 37\;\text{MeV}$.
