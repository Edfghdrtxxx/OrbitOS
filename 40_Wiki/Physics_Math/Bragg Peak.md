# Bragg Peak

## Definition

The Bragg peak is the sharp maximum in the [[dE-dx|energy loss]] ($\mathrm{d}E/\mathrm{d}x$) profile of a charged particle near the end of its range in matter. As the particle slows down, its energy loss per unit length increases according to the [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]], reaching a pronounced peak just before the particle stops.

## Key Points

- The peak height and position depend on the particle's charge $Z$, mass $A$, and initial kinetic energy — heavier and more charged particles produce larger, sharper peaks
- Bragg peak fitting in [[Time Projection Chamber|TPC]] [[Track Reconstruction|track reconstruction]] is used to determine the particle's range, stopping point, and identity ([[PID]])
- The characteristic shape (slow rise followed by sharp peak and rapid falloff) distinguishes stopped particles from punch-through particles that exit the detector
- Exploited in hadron therapy (proton/carbon therapy) to deliver maximum dose at tumour depth while sparing surrounding tissue

## Examples

- In the [[RCNP]] ATPC analysis, the Bragg peak region of reconstructed tracks is fitted to extract energy and identify the particle species, complementing [[PID]] from [[Silicon Detector|silicon detectors]] and [[Ionisation Chamber|ion chambers]]
- Proton therapy uses the Bragg peak to concentrate radiation dose precisely at the tumour location
