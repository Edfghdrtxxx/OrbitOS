## Definition

The Bragg curve is a plot of the [[Energy Resolution|energy loss]] (or ionization) of a charged particle as a function of its penetration depth in a medium. It is characterized by a relatively low and slowly rising energy loss along most of the particle's path, followed by a sharp maximum — the **Bragg peak** — near the end of the particle's range, after which the energy loss drops rapidly to zero.

## Key Points

- The shape is explained by the [[Bethe-Bloch Formula]]: energy loss $-dE/dx \propto 1/v^2$, so as the particle slows down, its energy loss per unit length increases dramatically
- The sharp **Bragg peak** makes charged-particle beams (especially protons and carbon ions) ideal for **radiation therapy**, since dose can be concentrated at tumour depth while sparing surrounding tissue
- Heavier ions (e.g. $^{12}\text{C}$) produce a sharper Bragg peak with less lateral scattering than protons
- A **spread-out Bragg peak (SOBP)** is created by superimposing beams of different energies to cover an extended tumour volume uniformly
- The range (depth of the Bragg peak) depends on particle type, initial kinetic energy, and the stopping power of the medium

## Examples

- In proton therapy, clinicians tune the beam energy so the Bragg peak coincides with the tumour location, delivering maximum dose to the target while minimizing dose to healthy tissue in front of and behind it
- In nuclear physics experiments using a [[Track Reconstruction|TPC]], the Bragg curve shape along a particle track is used for **particle identification** — different ion species produce distinct $dE/dx$ profiles
