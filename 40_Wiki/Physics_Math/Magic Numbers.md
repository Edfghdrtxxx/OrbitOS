# Magic Numbers

## Definition

Magic numbers are specific numbers of protons or neutrons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126) at which atomic nuclei exhibit exceptional stability. They arise from large energy gaps between filled and empty shells in the [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model]], caused by [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]] splitting.

## Key Points

- Nuclei with a magic number of protons or neutrons have higher binding energy per nucleon, lower neutron capture cross sections, and more stable isotopes than their neighbors
- Doubly magic nuclei (magic $Z$ and magic $N$) are extraordinarily stable: $^{4}$He, $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, $^{208}$Pb
- The first three magic numbers (2, 8, 20) can be explained by a simple [[Harmonic Oscillator]] potential, but 28, 50, 82, 126 require [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]]
- The [[Liquid Drop Model]] cannot account for magic numbers — this was a key motivation for the [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model]]
- In exotic nuclei far from stability, traditional magic numbers may erode and new ones can appear

## Examples

- $^{208}$Pb ($Z=82$, $N=126$) is the heaviest stable doubly magic nucleus, with a first excited state at 2.6 MeV
- Tin ($Z=50$) has 10 stable isotopes — more than any other element — reflecting the magic proton number
