# Scintillation Detector

## Definition

A scintillation detector is a radiation detector that converts [[Ionizing Radiation]] into visible light using a scintillating material, then collects and amplifies the light with a [[Photomultiplier Tube]] (or silicon photomultiplier) to produce a measurable electrical signal.

## Key Points

- **Scintillator types**: Inorganic crystals (NaI(Tl), CsI, BGO) offer high light yield and good energy resolution; organic scintillators (plastic, liquid) provide fast timing and neutron sensitivity
- **Signal chain**: Radiation → scintillation photons → photodetector → electrical pulse; pulse height is proportional to deposited energy
- **Applications**: [[Gamma Spectroscopy]], [[Time-of-Flight]] measurements, medical imaging (PET/SPECT), and environmental radiation monitoring
- **Key properties**: Light yield, decay time (determines timing resolution), and emission wavelength (must match photodetector sensitivity)

## Examples

- A NaI(Tl) crystal coupled to a [[Photomultiplier Tube]] is the standard setup for laboratory [[Gamma Spectroscopy]], identifying [[Radioisotope|radioisotopes]] by their characteristic gamma-ray energies
- Plastic scintillator arrays provide fast timing for [[Coincidence Detection]] and [[Time-of-Flight]] particle identification in beam-line experiments
