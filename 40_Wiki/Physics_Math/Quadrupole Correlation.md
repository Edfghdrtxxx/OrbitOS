# Quadrupole Correlation

## Schematics

![[Quadrupole_Correlation_schematic.png|742]]
*Prolate, spherical, and oblate nuclear shapes produced by quadrupole deformation. (CC0, Wikimedia Commons)*

## Definition

Quadrupole correlation is the collective proton-neutron interaction driven by the quadrupole ($\lambda = 2$) component of the nuclear residual interaction. It couples valence protons and neutrons to generate coherent $E2$ collectivity, lowering the energy of deformed configurations and driving nuclei away from spherical [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model|shell-closure]] shapes toward prolate or oblate equilibrium deformations.

## Key Points

- The dominant term is the proton-neutron quadrupole-quadrupole interaction $V_{p\text{-}n} \propto -\kappa\, Q_\pi \cdot Q_\nu$, where $Q_\pi$ and $Q_\nu$ are the quadrupole operators for protons and neutrons; the negative sign favors maximum overlap (deformation)
- Quadrupole correlations compete with the [[40_Wiki/Physics_Math/Nuclear Shell Model|shell gap]] energy: near [[Magic Numbers]], the gap wins and the nucleus stays spherical; far from closed shells, accumulated $p$-$n$ quadrupole strength overwhelms the gap and the ground state becomes deformed
- The product $N_\pi N_\nu$ (number of valence proton and neutron pairs outside the nearest closed shell) is an empirical scaling variable — observables like $B(E2; 0^+ \to 2^+)$ and $E(2^+_1)$ correlate smoothly with $N_\pi N_\nu$ across entire regions of the nuclear chart (Casten's $N_pN_n$ scheme)
- In the [[Interacting Boson Model]] (IBM), quadrupole correlations enter through the $d$-boson ($L = 2$) degree of freedom; the balance between pairing ($s$-boson) and quadrupole ($d$-boson) terms governs the transition between spherical, $\gamma$-soft, and axially-deformed limits
- When two configurations with different deformations coexist at similar energies, the result is [[Deformation Coexistence|shape coexistence]] — a direct consequence of competing shell stabilization and quadrupole correlation energy

## Examples

- In the rare-earth region ($Z \approx 64$-$72$, $N \approx 90$-$104$), large $N_\pi N_\nu$ values produce some of the most collectively deformed nuclei, with $B(E2)$ values exceeding 200 W.u. and low-lying rotational bands
- The rapid onset of deformation at $N = 60$ in the Zr isotopes ($^{100}$Zr) reflects a sudden gain in quadrupole correlation energy as neutrons fill the $g_{7/2}$-$d_{5/2}$ orbitals far from the $N = 50$ closure
