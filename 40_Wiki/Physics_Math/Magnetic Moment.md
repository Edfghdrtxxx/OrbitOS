## Definition

The magnetic moment $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is a vector quantity that characterizes the strength and orientation of a magnetic source, determining the torque $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \mathbf{B}$ it experiences in an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$. For a current loop, $\mu = IA$ where $I$ is the current and $A$ is the enclosed area; for quantum particles, it arises from intrinsic spin and orbital angular momentum.

## Key Points

- **Orbital magnetic moment:** For a charged particle with orbital angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$, the magnetic moment is $\boldsymbol{\mu}_l = -g_l \frac{e}{2m} \mathbf{L}$, where $g_l$ is the orbital g-factor ($g_l = 1$ for a free particle).
- **Spin magnetic moment:** An intrinsic contribution from particle spin $\mathbf{S}$: $\boldsymbol{\mu}_s = -g_s \frac{e}{2m} \mathbf{S}$, with $g_s \approx 2$ for electrons (Dirac prediction) and anomalous values for composite particles like protons ($g_p \approx 5.586$) and neutrons ($g_n \approx -3.826$).
- **Nuclear magneton:** The natural unit for nuclear magnetic moments is $\mu_N = \frac{e\hbar}{2m_p} \approx 5.051 \times 10^{-27}$ J/T, analogous to the [[Bohr Magneton]] $\mu_B$ for electrons.
- **Schmidt lines:** Single-particle estimates of nuclear magnetic moments based on the [[Nuclear Shell Model]] predict values along two Schmidt lines (for $j = l + \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $j = l - \tfrac{1}{2}$), but measured values typically fall between them due to many-body correlations.
- **Measurement techniques:** Nuclear magnetic moments are measured via NMR, laser spectroscopy, or $\beta$-NMR, providing sensitive probes of nuclear structure far from stability.

## Examples

- The proton has a magnetic moment of $\mu_p \approx 2.793\,\mu_N$, significantly deviating from the Dirac prediction of $1\,\mu_N$, revealing its composite quark substructure.
- The anomalous magnetic moment of the electron, $(g-2)/2 \approx 1.16 \times 10^{-3}$, is one of the most precisely measured quantities in physics, serving as a stringent test of quantum electrodynamics.
