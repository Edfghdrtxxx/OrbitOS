# Neutron Capture Cross Section

## Definition

The neutron capture cross section (σ) is the effective target area a nucleus presents for capturing a neutron, measured in barns (1 b = 10⁻²⁴ cm²). A larger cross section means the nucleus is more likely to undergo [[Neutron Activation]].

## Key Points

- Depends strongly on neutron energy — many nuclei show large resonance peaks at specific energies
- Thermal neutrons (~0.025 eV) often have much larger capture cross sections than fast neutrons, which is why moderators increase activation rates
- Related to nuclear structure: nuclei near [[Shell closure]] (magic numbers) tend to have smaller cross sections because their tightly bound configurations are less receptive to additional neutrons
- Tabulated in nuclear databases (ENDF, JENDL) and used to predict [[Neutron Activation]] yields in shielding and material studies

## Examples

- ¹⁹⁷Au has σ ≈ 99 barns for thermal neutrons — gold foils are used as activation detectors precisely because of this large cross section
- ⁵⁶Fe has σ ≈ 2.6 barns for thermal capture — relatively low, but still enough to produce measurable activation in steel near beamlines
