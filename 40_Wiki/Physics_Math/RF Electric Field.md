# RF Electric Field

## Definition

An RF (radio-frequency) electric field is a time-oscillating electric field, typically in the MHz–GHz range, used inside accelerating structures (cavities, dees, gaps) to repeatedly kick charged particles to higher energies. The field reverses polarity in sync with the particles' arrival so that they always see an accelerating voltage.

## Key Points

- In a [[Cyclotron]], an RF voltage is applied across the gap between two D-shaped electrodes; particles cross the gap twice per revolution, gaining energy $\Delta E = qV_\text{RF}\cos\phi$ each crossing, where $\phi$ is the phase relative to the RF peak
- The resonance condition requires $f_\text{RF}$ to match the cyclotron frequency (or a harmonic) — if the particle orbit period drifts (e.g., relativistic effects), acceleration stalls unless corrected by an [[Isochronous Cyclotron]] design or frequency ramping ([[Synchrotron]])
- In a [[Synchrotron]], RF cavities provide longitudinal focusing via phase stability (particles arriving early/late see restoring kicks), forming bunched beams
- Typical accelerating voltages range from $\sim 10\;\text{kV}$ per gap (cyclotrons) to $\sim 1\;\text{MV}$ per cavity (synchrotrons, linacs)

## Examples

- **Cyclotron dee voltage:** A classical [[Cyclotron]] with $50\;\text{kV}$ across the gap at $\sim 15\;\text{MHz}$ accelerates protons by $100\;\text{keV}$ per revolution (two crossings)
- **Synchrotron RF cavity:** The LHC uses superconducting RF cavities at $400\;\text{MHz}$ delivering $2\;\text{MV}$ per cavity to maintain proton bunches
