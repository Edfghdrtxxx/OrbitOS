# Knockout Reactions

## Definition

Knockout reactions are [[Direct Reactions]] in which a nucleon is ejected from the projectile (or target) by a sudden collision with a target (or projectile) nucleon, leaving the residual nucleus in a specific final state. They directly probe the single-particle occupancy of nuclear orbitals.

## Key Points

- $(p,2p)$ and $(p,pn)$ are the most common knockout channels — a proton target knocks out a proton or neutron from the beam nucleus
- Performed primarily in inverse kinematics at intermediate energies ($\sim$200–300 MeV/u) at facilities like RIBF ([[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN]]) and GSI
- The measured inclusive cross section, compared to eikonal or [[DWBA]]-based reaction theory, yields [[Spectroscopic Factor|spectroscopic factors]] — often showing stronger [[Quenching Problem|quenching]] than [[Transfer Reactions]]
- Momentum distributions of the residue encode the orbital angular momentum $\ell$ of the removed nucleon
- Complementary to [[Transfer Reactions]]: knockout is most effective for deeply-bound states, while transfer excels for weakly-bound states

## Examples

- $^{14}\text{O}(p,2p)^{13}\text{N}$ at RIBF: proton knockout measuring proton occupancy in a proton-rich nucleus
- $(e,e'p)$: electromagnetic knockout — the cleanest probe (no nuclear distortions), performed at JLab and previously at NIKHEF
