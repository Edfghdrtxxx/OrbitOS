# Nuclear Shell Model

## Definition

The nuclear shell model describes atomic nuclei as systems of nucleons (protons and neutrons) occupying discrete energy levels within a [[Mean-Field Potential]], analogous to electron shells in atoms. The inclusion of [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]] correctly reproduces the observed [[Magic Numbers]] and explains nuclear ground-state spins, parities, and magnetic moments.

## Key Points

- Nucleons fill energy levels according to the Pauli exclusion principle, with each level labeled by [[Quantum Numbers]] $(n, l, j)$ and holding $2j+1$ nucleons
- A [[Harmonic Oscillator]] potential plus spin-orbit term $V_{so}\,\vec{L}\cdot\vec{S}$ gives the correct level ordering; more realistic potentials (Woods-Saxon) refine the picture
- Shell closures at [[Magic Numbers]] (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) correspond to large energy gaps between filled and empty levels
- The ground-state spin and parity of odd-$A$ nuclei are determined by the single unpaired nucleon (single-particle model)
- Developed independently by Maria Goeppert Mayer and J. Hans D. Jensen (Nobel Prize 1963)

## Examples

- $^{17}$O has one neutron beyond the $^{16}$O doubly-magic core in the $1d_{5/2}$ orbital, giving ground-state spin-parity $5/2^+$
- $^{208}$Pb is doubly magic ($Z=82$, $N=126$), making it exceptionally stable with a high first excited state energy
