# Isochronous Cyclotron

## Definition

An isochronous cyclotron is a variant of the [[Cyclotron]] that maintains constant orbital frequency at relativistic energies by using a radially increasing magnetic field $B(r)$ to compensate the relativistic mass increase $\gamma m$, keeping $f_c = qB(r)/(2\pi \gamma m)$ constant.

## Key Points

- In a classical [[Cyclotron]], the resonance condition $f_\text{RF} = qB/(2\pi m)$ breaks down as particles become relativistic because $\gamma$ increases with energy — the orbit period lengthens and particles slip out of phase with the fixed-frequency RF
- The isochronous fix: shape the magnetic field so $B(r) \propto \gamma(r)$, restoring constant revolution frequency at all radii — this allows continuous-wave (CW) beam extraction with high average current
- Azimuthal field variations (sector or spiral poles) provide vertical focusing, which the radially increasing $B(r)$ alone would destroy — hence the alternative name **sector-focused cyclotron**
- Modern heavy-ion facilities rely on isochronous cyclotrons: [[HIRFL]] at [[Institute of Modern Physics]], [[Superconducting Ring Cyclotron]] at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN]]

## Examples

- **HIRFL-CSR injector:** The sector-focused cyclotron SFC at [[HIRFL]] accelerates heavy ions before injection into the cooler-storage rings
- **RIKEN SRC:** The [[Superconducting Ring Cyclotron]] is the world's most powerful isochronous cyclotron for [[Radioactive Isotope Beam]] production via [[Projectile Fragmentation]]
