# Spectral Lines

## Definition

Spectral lines are discrete wavelengths of light emitted or absorbed when electrons transition between quantized energy levels in atoms or molecules. Each element has a unique spectral fingerprint determined by its electronic structure and [[Quantum Numbers]].

## Key Points

- Emission lines appear when an electron drops to a lower energy level, releasing a photon with energy $E = h\nu = E_i - E_f$; absorption lines occur in the reverse process
- [[Fine Structure]] splitting (from [[Spin-Orbit Coupling]]) produces closely spaced doublets or multiplets within a single spectral line
- Selection rules govern allowed transitions: $\Delta l = \pm 1$, $\Delta j = 0, \pm 1$ (but $j=0 \to j=0$ forbidden) for electric dipole radiation
- Line broadening mechanisms include natural (lifetime), Doppler (thermal motion), and pressure (collisional) broadening
- Spectral analysis is the primary tool for determining the composition and physical conditions of stars and interstellar gas

## Examples

- The hydrogen Balmer series ($n \to 2$ transitions) produces visible lines: H-$\alpha$ (656.3 nm, red), H-$\beta$ (486.1 nm, blue-green)
- The sodium D-line doublet (589.0 / 589.6 nm) results from [[Fine Structure]] splitting of the $3p$ state
