# -sent- Root (sentire)

## Definition

The cluster of English words containing **-sent-** traces back to the Latin verb **sentire** ("to feel, perceive, sense"). Each prefix reshapes the core meaning of "feeling" in a different direction. Not all words spelled with *-sent-* share this root — some (like *present*, *absent*) derive from Latin *esse* ("to be") and are false cognates.

## Key Points

- **consent** (con- "together" + sentire) — to feel together, i.e., to agree. "They consented to the plan."
- **resent** (re- "intensely/back" + Old French *sentir* < sentire) — to feel bitterly about something done to you. "She resented being overlooked."
- **dissent** (dis- "apart" + sentire) — to feel differently, to disagree. "Three judges dissented."
- **assent** (ad- "toward" + sentire) — to feel favorably toward, to agree. "He nodded his assent."
- **sentence** (sententia "opinion" < sentire) — originally meant "an opinion or judgment"; evolved into both the legal and grammatical senses.

## Examples

| Word | Prefix | Literal sense | Modern meaning |
|------|--------|--------------|----------------|
| consent | con- (together) | feel together | agree, give permission |
| resent | re- (back/intensely) | feel back at | feel indignation |
| dissent | dis- (apart) | feel apart | disagree |
| assent | ad- (toward) | feel toward | agree, approve |
| sentiment | — (direct) | a feeling | opinion, emotional attitude |

**False friends** (contain *-sent-* but derive from *esse* "to be"):
- *present* (prae- + esse = "being before")
- *absent* (ab- + esse = "being away")
- *represent* (re- + praesentare)
