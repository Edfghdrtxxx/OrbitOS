# Conditional Sentences

## Definition

Sentences with an **if-clause** (condition) and a **main clause** (result), expressing real or hypothetical situations. English has four standard types plus mixed conditionals, each using specific tense combinations to signal how real or likely the condition is.

## Key Points

- **Zero conditional (general truth):** If + present simple, present simple — "If you heat water to 100°C, it boils." (always true)
- **First conditional (real future):** If + present simple, will + base verb — "If I pass TOEFL, I **will** apply to UTokyo." (possible and likely)
- **Second conditional (unreal present/future):** If + past simple, would + base verb — "If I **spoke** Japanese fluently, I **would** apply directly." (hypothetical now)
- **Third conditional (unreal past):** If + past perfect, would have + past participle — "If I **had started** earlier, I **would have finished** by now." (impossible to change)
- **Mixed conditional (past → present):** If + past perfect, would + base verb — "If I **had chosen** IPA, I **would be** in a different situation now."
- **Common error:** Using "will" in the if-clause — ~~"If I will pass"~~ → "If I **pass**"

## Examples

- "If the detector **works** properly, we **will** collect good data." (first — real possibility)
- "If I **were** you, I **would** focus on TOEFL first." (second — hypothetical advice; note "were" for all subjects in formal usage)
- "If she **had known** about the deadline, she **would have** applied." (third — regret about the past)
