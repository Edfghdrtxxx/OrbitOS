# Gratitude

## Definition

A deep, conscious appreciation for something received — not transactional thanks but reflective recognition of value, implying awareness of a source. From Latin *grātus* ("pleasing, thankful"), ultimately from PIE root *\*gʷerH-* ("to praise").

## Key Points

- **Etymology:** Latin *grātus* was bidirectional — it meant both "pleasing" (outward) and "thankful" (inward). English split this into two branches: *grateful* (thankful direction) and *gracious* (pleasing direction). The noun *gratitude* came via Medieval Latin *grātitūdō* to name the inner state. Same word family as *grace*, *grateful*, *gratis*, *agree*.
- **Weight scale:** *thanks* (light, routine) → *appreciation* (moderate, conscious) → *gratitude* (deep, reflective). Gratitude sits at the heaviest end — it implies sustained awareness, not a momentary reaction.
- **Collocations:** express / show gratitude, feel gratitude, gratitude toward / for (someone or something), with gratitude, a sense of gratitude.
- **Register:** Formal and reflective. Common in academic writing (see [[Academic_Word_List_AWL]]), personal essays, and mindfulness contexts. Rarely used in casual speech where *thanks* or *appreciation* suffice.

## Examples

- "She felt deep gratitude toward her mentor for years of patient guidance." (inner state, reflective)
- "He expressed his gratitude with a handwritten letter." (outward expression of the inner state)
