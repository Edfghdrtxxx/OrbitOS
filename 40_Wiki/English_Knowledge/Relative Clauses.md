# Relative Clauses

## Definition

A clause introduced by a relative pronoun (**who, which, that, whom, whose**) or relative adverb (**where, when**) that modifies a noun by providing additional information about it. They come in two types: **defining** (essential) and **non-defining** (extra info).

## Key Points

- **Defining (no commas):** Identifies which noun we mean — "The professor **who supervised my thesis** is at UTokyo." (without the clause, we don't know which professor)
- **Non-defining (with commas):** Adds extra info about an already-identified noun — "Dr. Tanaka, **who works at RIKEN**, gave a lecture." (we already know who; the clause is bonus info)
- **That vs. which:** In defining clauses, "that" and "which" are interchangeable; in non-defining clauses, only "which" is allowed — ~~"RIKEN, that is in Wako,"~~ → "RIKEN, **which** is in Wako,"
- **Omitting the pronoun:** In defining clauses, when the pronoun is the **object**, it can be dropped — "The paper (that) I read" is fine; but not when it's the subject — ~~"The paper was published"~~ needs "The paper **that** was published"
- **Reduced relative clauses:** Common in academic writing — "The detector **used** in this experiment" (= "which was used")

## Examples

- "Students **who score above 90** receive scholarships." (defining — which students?)
- "The TPC, **which was developed in the 1970s**, is widely used." (non-defining — extra info)
- "The city **where** I was born is in Shandong." (relative adverb)
