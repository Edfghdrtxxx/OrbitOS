# Where vs That (Relative Clauses)

## Definition

In [[Relative Clauses]], the choice between **"that"** and **"where"** depends on the grammatical role the antecedent plays inside the clause: use **"that"** when the antecedent is a subject or object (a thing/person), and **"where"** when the antecedent serves as a location or setting (replaceable with "in which").

## Key Points

- **Decision test:** Ask what role the antecedent plays *inside* the clause — is it a thing being acted on / doing the action, or a place/setting in which the action happens?
- **"that"** = antecedent is the **subject or object** of the clause verb (e.g., "A system **that** scales well" — *system* is the subject of *scales*)
- **"where"** = antecedent is a **location or setting** (concrete or abstract) — always replaceable with "in which" (e.g., "The lab **where** I work" = "The lab in which I work")
- **Common mistake:** The "different subject" heuristic (if the clause has a different subject from the antecedent, use "where") is **incomplete** — it fails on object clauses like "The book **that** I read," where the subjects differ but "that" is correct because *book* is an object, not a place

| Antecedent's role | Use |
|---|---|
| Subject or object (a thing) | **that** |
| Location / setting ("in which") | **where** |

## Examples

- "A system **that** scales well" — *system* = subject of "scales" → **that**
- "The book **that** I read" — *book* = object of "read" → **that**
- "The lab **where** I work" — *lab* = place "in which" I work → **where**
- "A situation **where** nobody wins" — *situation* = abstract setting "in which" nobody wins → **where**
