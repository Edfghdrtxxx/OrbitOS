# Premise vs Prerequisite

## Definition

A **premise** is a statement or assumption that forms the basis of an argument or line of reasoning. A **prerequisite** is something that must exist or happen before something else can occur. They are often confused because both relate to "things that come before," but premise is about *logic* while prerequisite is about *requirements*.

## Key Points

- **Premise** = a proposition assumed to be true in order to draw a conclusion (e.g., "The premise of his argument is that all matter is composed of atoms")
- **Prerequisite** = a condition that must be met before proceeding (e.g., "Calculus is a prerequisite for this physics course")
- Common error: using "premise" when meaning "prerequisite" — e.g., ~~"Understanding X is a premise of the discussion"~~ should be "Understanding X is a **prerequisite** for the discussion"
- Preposition pairing: premise **of** an argument; prerequisite **for** an action

## Examples

- *Premise:* "The premise of the experiment is that particles behave differently at high energies."
- *Prerequisite:* "Passing the [[GRE Physics]] is a prerequisite for admission to the program."
- Wrong: "Basic math is a premise of this course." → Right: "Basic math is a **prerequisite** for this course."
