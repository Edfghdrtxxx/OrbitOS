# English Collocations

## Definition

A collocation is a pair or group of words that habitually appear together and sound "natural" to native speakers. Using the wrong combination is grammatically permissible but sounds awkward or unnatural — a key signal of non-native speech.

## Key Points

- **Verb + preposition collocations:** Many verbs require specific prepositions that cannot be predicted from meaning alone — "teach someone **about** X" (not "teach someone X" when X is a concrete noun), "explain X **to** someone" (not "explain someone X")
- **Naming collocations:** "called" is the everyday default ("a detector **called** MUSIC"), "named" is slightly formal, "**known as**" is the academic/formal register — all are correct, but register differs
- **Common non-native errors:**
  - ~~"Teach me a detector"~~ → "**Tell me about** a detector" or "**Teach me about** a detector" — "teach" + direct concrete noun implies skill transfer, not information
  - ~~"Explain me this"~~ → "**Explain this to me**" — "explain" cannot take an indirect object directly
- **Why collocations matter:** Native speakers rarely notice correct collocations, but wrong ones immediately stand out. Learning collocations is more effective than memorising grammar rules for sounding natural

## Examples

- "**Make** a decision" (not ~~do a decision~~)
- "**Take** a photo" (not ~~make a photo~~)
- "**Do** research" (not ~~make research~~)
- "**Have** an experience" (not ~~do an experience~~)
