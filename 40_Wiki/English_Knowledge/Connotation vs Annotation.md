# Connotation vs Annotation

## Definition

Two visually similar but unrelated English words sharing the Latin root **notāre** (to mark): a **connotation** is an implied feeling or association a word carries beyond its literal meaning, while an **annotation** is a note or comment added to a text to explain or mark something.

## Key Points

- **Shared root:** Latin *notāre* (to mark/note) — the prefix makes the difference
- **Connotation** from *con-* (with/together) + *notāre* → "to mark together with" → a meaning that travels **with** the word beneath the surface (e.g., "cheap" literally = low cost, but *connotes* poor quality)
- **Annotation** from *ad-* (to/toward) + *notāre* → "to mark onto" → a note directed **at** a specific passage (e.g., margin notes, code `@annotations`)
- **Memory trick:** **con**notation = meaning **con**cealed inside a word; **an**notation = a note **an**chored to a text

## Examples

- *The word "home" has warm connotations of safety and belonging, while "house" is neutral.*
- *The professor's annotations in the margins helped me understand the difficult passage.*
