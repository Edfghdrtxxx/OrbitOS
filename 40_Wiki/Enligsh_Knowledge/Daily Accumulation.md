## Details
### buffer weeks
- Meaning: Extra weeks reserved to absorb delays.
- Example: We added two buffer weeks before the conference deadline.

### buffer
- Meaning: A person or thing that prevents conflict or reduces impact.
- Example: A savings fund can act as a buffer during uncertain times.

### screening
- Meaning: The process of filtering and selecting candidates or items.
- Example: After initial screening, only five candidates were shortlisted.

### nominate
- Root: *nom-* (Latin/Greek: name) → nominate, nominal, denomination, anonymous, synonym, taxonomy
- Meaning: To formally suggest someone for a role, award, or election.
- Example: They nominated her for the best student paper award.

### diligent / diligence
- Root: *dis-* (Latin: apart) + *legere* (Latin: to choose/pick) → diligent, select, neglect, collect, elect, intellect, eligible, lecture
- Meaning: diligent (adj.) hard-working; diligence (n.) careful and persistent effort.
- Example: Her diligent study habits show great diligence.

### Dirac notation
- Meaning: Bra-ket notation used in quantum mechanics.
- Example: The state can be written as |ψ⟩ in Dirac notation.

### due
- Meaning: Expected, owed, or scheduled at a specific time.
- Example: The report is due on Monday.

### drain / draining
- Meaning: drain (v.) to use up energy, money, or resources; draining (adj.) making someone feel very tired.
- Example: Long meetings can drain your focus and feel mentally draining.

### self-fulfilling prophecy
- Meaning: A prediction or belief that causes behaviors which make it come true.
- Example: If you keep saying you will fail and stop trying, that belief can become a self-fulfilling prophecy.

### antagonistic
- Root: *anti-* (Greek: against) + *agon-* (Greek: contest) → antagonist, protagonist, agonize, agony
- Meaning: Showing opposition, hostility, or conflict toward someone or something.
- Example: Their antagonistic attitude made collaboration difficult.

### sunk-cost trap
- Meaning: A bias where people keep investing time, money, or effort because of past investment, even when quitting is the better choice.
- Example: I stayed in the project too long because I fell into the sunk-cost trap.

### hedge
- Meaning: hedge (v.) to reduce risk by taking an offsetting action; hedge (n.) a strategy used to limit potential loss (also: a row of bushes).
- Example: Investors often hedge their positions to protect against market volatility.

### frame / framing
- Meaning: frame (v.) to present or structure an idea in a particular way; framing (n.) the way information is presented to shape interpretation.
- Example: How you frame the problem can strongly influence the team's decision.

### blunt
- Meaning: blunt (adj.) direct and sometimes insensitive in speech; also not sharp. blunt (v.) to make less sharp or to reduce force/effect.
- Example: His blunt feedback was hard to hear, but it helped improve the draft.

### span
- Meaning: In linear algebra, the span of a set of vectors is the set of all linear combinations of those vectors (the space they can generate).
- Example: The vectors (1, 0) and (0, 1) span the entire 2D plane.

### commutativity
- Meaning: The property that changing the order of an operation does not change the result (e.g., a + b = b + a).
- Derivables: commutative (adj.), noncommutative (adj.).
- Example: Addition is commutative, but matrix multiplication is not.

### echelon
- Meaning: A level or rank within an organization or system.
- Example: She quickly moved into the upper echelons of the lab's leadership.

### diagonal
- Root: *dia-* (Greek: through/across) + *gonia* (Greek: angle) → diagonal, diameter, diagnosis, dialogue, polygon, hexagon, pentagon
- Meaning: A line connecting two non-adjacent corners of a shape; also describing something slanted or at an angle.
- Example: The rectangle's diagonal is longer than its side.

### tedious
- Meaning: Too long, slow, or repetitive; boring.
- Example: Manually labeling thousands of images is tedious work.

### perpendicular vs vertical
- Root: *per-* (Latin: through) + *pendere* (Latin: to hang) → perpendicular, pendant, depend, suspend, pendulum, expenditure, compensate
- Core difference: **perpendicular** describes a 90° relationship between two lines/planes; **vertical** describes an up-and-down direction relative to the horizon.
- Use cases: "The lines are perpendicular." (relationship) vs "The line is vertical." (orientation).
- Example: The z-axis is vertical; the x- and y-axes are perpendicular.

### eigenstate
- Meaning: A state that remains unchanged (up to a scalar factor) when acted on by a given operator.
- Example: An energy eigenstate evolves only by a phase factor in time.

### coefficients vs factor
- Core difference: **coefficients** are the numbers multiplying variables/terms; **factor** is any quantity that contributes to a result (a multiplier or a component).
- Use cases: "The coefficient of x is 3." vs "Temperature is a key factor in the reaction rate."
- Example: In 3x + 2y, the coefficients are 3 and 2; in a product ab, both a and b are factors.

### homogeneous / non-homogeneous / homogeneous linear constraint
- Root: *homo-* (Greek: same) + *genos* (Greek: kind/origin) → homogeneous, heterogeneous, homologous, homonym; *genos* also: genre, genocide, general, gender
- Meaning: homogeneous (adj.) all terms are of the same degree; non-homogeneous (adj.) not all terms are of the same degree or includes a nonzero constant term; homogeneous linear constraint (n. phrase) a linear constraint with zero constant term (e.g., Ax = 0).
- Example: x + y = 0 is a homogeneous linear constraint; x + y = 1 is non-homogeneous.

### foolproof
- Meaning: Designed to be simple and safe so it cannot easily go wrong.
- Example: The setup guide is foolproof, even for beginners.

### conflate
- Root: *con-* (Latin: together) + *flare* (Latin: to blow) → conflate, inflate, deflate, flatulent
- Meaning: To combine or treat two or more things as if they were the same, often incorrectly.
- Example: Don't conflate correlation with causation.

### overhaul
- Meaning: overhaul (v.) to examine and repair or improve something thoroughly; overhaul (n.) a major repair or complete change.
- Example: The lab plans to overhaul its data pipeline before the next run.

### substantiate
- Root: *sub-* (Latin: under) + *stare* (Latin: to stand) → substance, substantial, substantiate, understand, circumstance, obstacle, instant, constant, contrast
- Meaning: To provide evidence or proof to support a claim, theory, or argument; to give solid grounds for something.
- Derivables: substantiated (adj.), unsubstantiated (adj.), substantiation (n.).
- Example: You need concrete data to substantiate your hypothesis before submitting the paper.

### populate / popular
- Root: *populus* (Latin: people) → populate, populous, population, popular
- Semantic evolution: **populate** originally meant "to people/settle" a place; it broadened to "fill with items/organisms/data." **popular** originally meant "of the people/public"; it later shifted to "liked/accepted by many people."
- Meaning: populate (v.) to fill with people/organisms/data; popular (adj.) liked by many; of the people.
- Example: The lab will populate the database with validated records; the new course became popular across departments.

### notation
- Root: *nota* (Latin: mark/sign) → note, denote, notation, annotate
- Meaning: A system of symbols or marks; the act of writing notes.
- Example: Dirac notation represents states with |ψ⟩ and ⟨ψ|.

### contamination
- Meaning: The presence of something harmful, dirty, or unwanted; pollution or impurity.
- Example: The lab shut down the experiment due to contamination in the sample.

### granularity
- Meaning: The level of detail or size of the units in data, analysis, or a system.
- Example: We need finer granularity to see day-by-day trends.

### escalate / escalation / de-escalate
- Root: *-scal-* (Latin: *scala* — ladder) → scale, escalator, echelon
- Meaning: **escalate** (v.) to increase rapidly in intensity or seriousness; **escalation** (n.) an increase in level or intensity; **de-escalate** (v.) to reduce the intensity of a conflict.
- Example: The minor dispute quickly escalated into a major argument; the police tried to de-escalate the situation.

### commit / commitment
- Root: *-mit-* (Latin: to send/put) → commit, transmit, submit, admit, emit
- Meaning: **commit** (v.) to pledge oneself to a path; to perform an action (often negative); to store data. **commitment** (n.) a state of being dedicated; a promise; a scheduled professional/social obligation.
- Example: I **commit** to the UTokyo path because I have a long-term **commitment** to my research career.
- Usage Note: "I have a prior **commitment**" is a professional way to say you have a scheduled appointment.

### summit
- Root: *summus* (Latin: highest) — **Not** related to *-mit-* (to send).
- Meaning: The highest point of a mountain; a meeting of high-level leaders.
- Example: The professors held a **summit** to discuss the future of the TPC project.

### demographic
- Root: *demos* (Greek: people) + *-graphic* (Greek: writing/describing) → democracy, demography, epidemic, pandemic
- Meaning: demographic (n.) a particular sector of a population grouped by shared characteristics (age, income, etc.); demographic (adj.) relating to population statistics.
- Example: Japan's aging demographic is a key factor driving immigration policy reform.

### cortisol
- Root: *cortex* (Latin: bark/outer layer) + *-ol* (chemistry: alcohol/steroid suffix) → cortex, cortical, corticosteroid
- Meaning: A steroid hormone produced by the adrenal glands in response to stress; regulates metabolism, immune response, and the sleep-wake cycle. Often called the "stress hormone."
- Example: Chronic stress keeps cortisol levels elevated, which can disrupt sleep and weaken immunity.

### drowsy
- Meaning: Feeling sleepy and not fully alert; on the edge of falling asleep.
- Derivables: drowsiness (n.), drowsily (adv.).
- Example: The warm lecture hall made everyone drowsy after lunch.

### hyperarousal
- Root: *hyper-* (Greek: over/excessive) + *arousal* (to wake up/stir) → hyperactive, hypersensitive, hyperventilate
- Meaning: A state of heightened physiological and psychological alertness, often linked to stress, anxiety, or trauma; the nervous system stays "on" even when there is no threat.
- Example: Hyperarousal from chronic stress made it impossible for her to relax before bed.

### insomnia
- Root: *in-* (Latin: not) + *somnus* (Latin: sleep) → insomnia, somnolent, somnambulism
- Meaning: The persistent inability to fall asleep or stay asleep.
- Example: Stress from the deadline gave him insomnia for three nights straight.
