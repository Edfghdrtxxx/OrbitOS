# Gerund

## Definition

A verb form ending in **-ing** that functions as a **noun** in a sentence. Despite looking identical to the [[Present Participle]], a gerund occupies noun positions (subject, object, complement), not adjective or verb positions.

## Key Points

- **As subject:** "**Swimming** is good exercise."
- **As object of a verb:** "I enjoy **reading**." — certain verbs (enjoy, avoid, consider, suggest, mind) always take a gerund, not an [[Infinitive]]
- **After prepositions:** "She is interested in **learning** Japanese." — English always uses a gerund (not infinitive) after prepositions
- **Gerund vs. infinitive:** Some verbs change meaning: "I stopped **smoking**" (quit the habit) vs. "I stopped **to smoke**" (paused in order to smoke)
- **Possessive before gerund (formal):** "I appreciate **your helping** me" is more formal than "I appreciate **you helping** me"

## Examples

- "**Running** every morning improved his health." (subject)
- "Do you mind **opening** the window?" (object after *mind*)
- "He is good at **solving** problems." (after preposition *at*)
