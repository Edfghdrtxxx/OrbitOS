# Course (word meanings)

## Definition

**Course** is a high-frequency English word with multiple distinct meanings depending on context. Understanding its polysemy (see [[Polysemy]]) is essential for both academic reading and everyday conversation.

## Etymology & Semantic Evolution

### The Root

PIE \**kers-* ("to run") → Latin *currere* (to run) → Latin *cursus* (a running, a journey, a flow) → Old French *cors* (12c.) → Middle English *course* (c. 1300)

### The Semantic Tree

The original sense of "directed movement / running" branched into every modern meaning:

```
PIE *kers- ("to run")
  └─ Latin currere (to run) → cursus (a running, a journey)
       └─ Old French cors (12c.) → Middle English course (c. 1300)
            │
            ├─ PATH / DIRECTION (c. 1300)
            │    "the line along which something moves"
            │    → ship's course, golf course, course of action
            │
            ├─ SEQUENCE / ORDER (c. 1300)
            │    "things that run one after another"
            │    │
            │    ├─ STAGE IN A MEAL (late 14c.)
            │    │    dishes served in sequence
            │    │    → main course, three-course dinner
            │    │
            │    ├─ SERIES OF LESSONS (c. 1600)
            │    │    a planned sequence of study
            │    │    → physics course, online course
            │    │
            │    └─ NATURAL PROGRESSION (early 14c.)
            │         "the way things run their sequence"
            │         → "in the course of time"
            │         → "of course" (1540s: "by natural order" → "obviously")
            │
            └─ FLOW (mid-14c.)
                 water running through a channel
                 → watercourse
```

> [!note] The word never truly changed meaning — it **branched**. The original sense of "something running in a direction" was simply applied to new domains over the centuries. Each modern meaning is a metaphorical extension of that single idea.

### Word Family

Other English words descending from Latin *currere*:

| Word | Literal sense from *currere* |
| --- | --- |
| **current** | "running" (water, electricity, events) |
| **cursor** | "runner" (the thing that runs across your screen) |
| **curriculum** | "a running / a race" (the track of study you run through) |
| **occur** | "run toward" (*ob-* + *currere*) |
| **recur** | "run back" (*re-* + *currere*) |
| **discourse** | "run apart / around" (*dis-* + *cursus*) |
| **concourse** | "run together" (*con-* + *cursus*) |

## Key Points

### 1. A series of lessons / academic program

A structured sequence of instruction on a subject.

- *I enrolled in a physics course this semester.*
- *She completed a postgraduate course at UTokyo.*

### 2. A direction or route

The path along which something moves, or a planned route.

- *The ship changed course to avoid the storm.*
- *We need to decide on a course of action.*

### 3. A part of a meal

One of the successive dishes served during a meal.

- *The main course was grilled salmon.*
- *A three-course dinner is standard at formal events.*

### 4. A natural progression / passage of time

The way something develops or the duration over which it unfolds.

- *In the course of the investigation, new evidence emerged.*
- *The disease runs its course in about two weeks.*

### 5. An area for sport

A designated area or track where a sport takes place.

- *He played 18 holes on the golf course.*
- *The obstacle course tested every participant's endurance.*

### 6. "Of course" — naturally, certainly

A fixed expression used to affirm, agree, or indicate something is obvious.

- *Of course you can borrow my notes.*
- *She passed the exam — of course she did, she studied every day.*

## Common Collocations

| Collocation | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **in the course of** | during; throughout |
| **of course** | naturally; certainly |
| **stay the course** | persist despite difficulty |
| **in due course** | at the appropriate time; eventually |
| **course of action** | a plan or strategy to follow |

## Examples

- *In the course of writing my thesis, I discovered several overlooked datasets.* (meaning 4)
- *Of course, preparation is the key to passing the entrance exam.* (meaning 6)
- *She signed up for an online course on machine learning.* (meaning 1)
- *Stay the course — the results will come in due course.* (collocations)
