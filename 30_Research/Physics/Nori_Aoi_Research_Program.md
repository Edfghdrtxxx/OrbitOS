# Prof. Nori Aoi — Research Program

## 1. Prof. Nori Aoi — Overview

**Nori Aoi** (青井 考, read "Aoi Nori") is a Professor at the [[UTokyo CNS|Center for Nuclear Study (CNS)]], University of Tokyo, a position he has held since March 2025. He leads the **[[NUSPEQ]]** group and is a central figure in the [[SUNFLOWER]] collaboration for in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIKEN RIBF]].

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: What Does Aoi Actually Study?
> **Q:** In one sentence, what is Aoi's research about?
> **A:** He figures out the *shape* and *internal architecture* of atomic nuclei that don't exist naturally on Earth — nuclei with far too many neutrons — by smashing beams of these exotic nuclei into targets and reading the gamma-ray "fingerprints" they emit as they de-excite.
>
> Think of it this way: stable nuclei are like well-known buildings whose blueprints you already have. Exotic nuclei are buildings that only exist for a fraction of a second before collapsing. Aoi's technique is like photographing the building using a high-speed camera (gamma-ray detectors) the instant before it falls, then reconstructing the blueprint (nuclear level scheme) from those snapshots.
>
> The reason this matters: physicists thought they knew the "magic numbers" — special neutron/proton counts that make nuclei extra stable, like noble gases in chemistry. Aoi's experiments show that these magic numbers **change** when you go far from stability. Some vanish (N=20, N=28), and new ones appear (N=16, N=34). Understanding *why* teaches us about the fundamental forces binding nucleons together.

**See also:** [[Nobuaki_Imai_Research_Program|Prof. Nobuaki Imai — Research Program]] | [[Prof_Nori_Aoi|Professor Profile]]

## 2. NUSPEQ Group

**NUSPEQ** — *Nuclear Spectroscopy for Extreme Quantum system* — is Aoi's research group at [[UTokyo CNS]]. The Japanese name is 極限原子核構造研究グループ (Extreme Nuclear Structure Research Group).

### Research Program

The group investigates **properties of nuclei in extreme states** along two axes:

1. **Unstable nuclei** far from the [[Valley of Stability|valley of stability]] — neutron-rich isotopes accessible only through [[Radioactive Isotope Beam|RI beam]] production at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIBF]]
2. **Extremely deformed nuclei** — [[Superdeformation|superdeformed]] (axis ratio $\sim 2{:}1$) and hyperdeformed (axis ratio $\sim 3{:}1$) states

These are pursued through two complementary sub-programs:

**Intermediate Energy Nuclear Spectroscopy:**
- In-beam [[Gamma-Ray Spectroscopy|gamma-ray spectroscopy]] with fast RI beams at $\sim 100$–$300$ MeV/u
- [[Knockout Reactions|Knockout]], [[Inelastic Scattering|inelastic scattering]], and [[Projectile Fragmentation|fragmentation]] reactions
- Detectors: [[DALI2]], [[HiCARI]], [[GRAPE]]

**Low Energy Nuclear Spectroscopy:**
- Decelerated beams via [[OEDO]] ($10$–$50$ MeV/u)
- [[Transfer Reactions|Nucleon transfer]], [[Coulomb Excitation|sub-barrier Coulomb excitation]], [[Missing-Mass Spectroscopy|missing-mass spectroscopy]]
- Detectors: [[CAT-M]] active-target TPC, [[SHARAQ Spectrometer|SHARAQ]]

## 3. In-Beam Gamma-Ray Spectroscopy

### 3.1 Motivation

In-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy is the **core experimental technique** of Aoi's research program. It provides direct access to the [[Nuclear Level Scheme|level scheme]] — excitation energies, spin-parity assignments, and [[Transition Probability|transition probabilities]] — of exotic nuclei produced as fast RI beams at [[RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science (RNC)|RIBF]].

The fundamental challenge: exotic nuclei live for milliseconds or less and cannot be accumulated as targets. Instead, they are produced as high-velocity secondary beams via [[Projectile Fragmentation|projectile fragmentation]] or [[In-Flight Fission|in-flight fission]] at [[BigRIPS]], then impinge on a secondary reaction target. The resulting nuclear excited states de-excite by emitting gamma rays, which are detected by surrounding arrays.

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: Why Gamma Rays Tell You About Nuclear Shape
> **Q:** How does detecting gamma rays reveal the *shape* of a nucleus?
> **A:** When a nucleus is excited — say, by a collision — it settles back to its ground state by emitting gamma-ray photons. The *energy* of each gamma ray equals the energy difference between the levels it transitions between. So by measuring gamma-ray energies, you reconstruct the ladder of energy levels inside the nucleus.
>
> Now here's the key insight: the spacing of those energy levels depends on the nuclear shape. A perfectly **spherical** nucleus near a magic number has its first excited state at high energy (it's "stiff" — hard to excite). A **deformed** nucleus — shaped like a rugby ball — has a low-lying first excited $2^+$ state and a characteristic rotational pattern where $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 3.33$.
>
> So when Aoi measures a gamma ray at, say, 660 keV in ${}^{34}\text{Mg}$ and finds $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 3.2$, he can immediately say: "This nucleus is a well-deformed rotor" — even though ${}^{34}\text{Mg}$ only existed for a tiny fraction of a second in his experiment.
>
> The **transition probability** $B(E2)$ goes further: it quantifies *how strongly* the nucleus is deformed. A large $B(E2)$ means many protons participate in the collective deformation — unless, as in ${}^{16}\text{C}$, the neutrons deform while the protons barely move, revealing a new kind of "neutron-dominant" collectivity.

### 3.2 Detector Systems

#### DALI2 / DALI2+

The **[[DALI2]]** (*Detector Array for Low Intensity radiation 2*) is a NaI(Tl) scintillator array and the workhorse detector for fast-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy at RIBF. Aoi is a co-author on the DALI2 instrument paper.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Number of detectors | 186 NaI(Tl) crystals |
| Angular coverage | $\sim 15°$–$160°$ polar angle |
| Angular resolution | $\sim 6°$ FWHM |
| Energy resolution | $\sim 10\%$ at 1 MeV |
| Photopeak efficiency | $\sim 20\%$ at 1 MeV for fast nuclei |

DALI2 was used in many of Aoi's landmark experiments: ${}^{32}\text{Ne}$ spectroscopy, ${}^{54}\text{Ca}$ magic-number discovery, ${}^{42}\text{Si}$ deformation, and the island-of-inversion systematic studies.

#### HiCARI

**[[HiCARI]]** (*High-resolution Cluster Array at RIBF*) is a composite germanium detector array assembled from [[MINIBALL]], Super Clover, and Ge tracking detectors through an international collaboration (Japan, China, South Korea, Europe, USA). It provides **high energy resolution** compared to DALI2, enabling [[Doppler Shift|Doppler-shift]] lifetime measurements and resolving closely spaced gamma-ray lines.

HiCARI was used in the 2024 triaxial Zr spectroscopy and the 2025 Mo isotope spectroscopy — both recent highlights of the NUSPEQ program.

#### GRAPE

**[[GRAPE]]** (*Gamma-Ray detector Array with Position and Energy sensitivity*) is a position-sensitive germanium detector array developed at [[UTokyo CNS]]. Its position sensitivity enables precise [[Doppler Correction|Doppler correction]] for gamma rays emitted from nuclei moving at $v/c \sim 0.3$–$0.6$, and it operates in combination with the [[SHARAQ Spectrometer|SHARAQ]] spectrometer.

### 3.3 Experimental Technique

A typical in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy experiment at RIBF proceeds as follows:

1. **Beam production:** A primary beam (e.g., ${}^{48}\text{Ca}$, ${}^{238}\text{U}$) is accelerated by the [[RIBF]] cyclotron cascade to $\sim 345$ MeV/u and impinges on a production target
2. **Fragment separation:** [[BigRIPS]] selects the desired secondary RI beam via magnetic rigidity ($B\rho$), time-of-flight (TOF), and energy loss ($\Delta E$) measurements — event-by-event [[Particle Identification|particle identification]]
3. **Reaction:** The tagged RI beam hits a secondary target (Be, C, or liquid H$_2$) where the nucleus of interest is produced or excited via [[Knockout Reactions|knockout]], [[Inelastic Scattering|inelastic scattering]], or [[Projectile Fragmentation|fragmentation]]
4. **Gamma detection:** De-excitation gamma rays are detected by [[DALI2]] (or [[HiCARI]]/[[GRAPE]]) surrounding the target position
5. **Residue identification:** Reaction products are identified by the [[ZeroDegree]] spectrometer downstream
6. **Analysis:** Gamma-ray spectra are Doppler-corrected and gated on specific reaction channels; excitation energies, level schemes, and cross sections are extracted

## 4. Shell Evolution in Neutron-Rich Nuclei

### 4.1 Overview

A central theme of Aoi's career has been the experimental demonstration that the traditional nuclear [[Magic Numbers|magic numbers]] — 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126 — are **not universal constants** but evolve as a function of neutron and proton number. Far from stability, some magic numbers vanish while new ones emerge, driven by changes in the underlying nuclear forces.

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: Why Do Magic Numbers Change?
> **Q:** If magic numbers come from the shell model, how can they change?
> **A:** In the textbook shell model, you solve for single-particle orbits in a fixed average potential (like a harmonic oscillator + spin-orbit term). The resulting energy gaps define magic numbers. But this picture assumes the potential doesn't change — which is only true near the valley of stability where the model was fitted.
>
> Far from stability, the **residual interaction between valence protons and neutrons** reshapes the single-particle energies. The key player is the **monopole component of the tensor force** — a part of the nucleon-nucleon interaction that shifts orbital energies depending on which other orbitals are occupied. Specifically, if you have a proton in an orbital $j_\pi = l + 1/2$ (spin aligned) and a neutron in $j_\nu = l' - 1/2$ (spin anti-aligned), the tensor force is **attractive**, pulling the neutron orbital down in energy. Remove those protons (as you do in neutron-rich nuclei), and the neutron orbital pops back up — potentially closing or opening a shell gap.
>
> This is why N=20 collapses in Ne and Mg (too few protons in the $d_{5/2}$ orbital to maintain the gap) and why N=34 appears in Ca (the $f_{5/2}$–$p_{1/2}$ gap opens up due to three-nucleon forces). The magic numbers are not broken — the forces that create them simply produce different gaps in different neighborhoods of the nuclear chart.

The effective single-particle energy shift is governed by the monopole interaction:

$$\epsilon_j^{\text{eff}} = \epsilon_j^{\text{bare}} + \sum_{j'} V_{\text{mono}}(j, j') \, n_{j'}$$

where $n_{j'}$ is the occupation number of orbital $j'$ and $V_{\text{mono}}$ encodes the monopole part of the two-body interaction.

### 4.2 Island of Inversion (N=20 Region)

The **[[Island of Inversion]]** is a region of the nuclear chart around N=20, Z=10–12 (Ne, Na, Mg) where nuclei exhibit unexpected ground-state deformation. The N=20 [[Shell Closure|shell closure]] collapses: intruder configurations (neutrons promoted from the $sd$-shell across the N=20 gap into the $fp$-shell) become energetically favored over the normal closed-shell configuration.

**Observable signatures:** Low $E(2^+)$ excitation energies, large $B(E2)$ values, $E(4^+)/E(2^+)$ ratios approaching the rotational limit of $10/3 \approx 3.33$.

#### Key Results

**${}^{34}\text{Mg}$: First Spectroscopy, Strong Deformation** (Aoi first author)
- N. Aoi et al., *Nucl. Phys. A* **701**, 133c (2002)
- First determination of low-lying states in ${}^{34}\text{Mg}$ via in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy with two-step fragmentation of a ${}^{36}\text{Si}$ beam at 38 MeV/u
- Two gamma lines at **660(10) keV** ($2^+ \to 0^+$) and **1460(20) keV** ($4^+ \to 2^+$)
- $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 3.2$ — consistent with a well-deformed rotor

**${}^{32}\text{Ne}$: Island Extends to N=22**
- P. Doornenbal, H. Scheit, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* **103**, 032501 (2009) — 96 citations
- First spectroscopic study of ${}^{32}\text{Ne}$ at RIBF; gamma-ray line at **722(9) keV** observed via both inelastic scattering (226 MeV/u on C) and proton removal from ${}^{33}\text{Na}$
- Confirms the island of inversion extends to at least N=22 for Ne isotopes

**${}^{32}\text{Mg}$: Proton Inelastic Scattering**
- Proton inelastic scattering at 56 MeV/u on a liquid hydrogen target, measuring de-excitation gamma-rays and extracting deformation lengths
- ${}^{32}\text{Mg}$ exhibits the largest quadrupole collectivity among the neutron-rich N=20 isotones

### 4.3 Merging of N=20 and N=28 Islands

In the neutron-rich Mg isotopic chain, the traditional shell closures at both N=20 and N=28 break down, creating a continuous region of deformation that merges the two "islands of inversion."

**${}^{34,36,38}\text{Mg}$: Merging Shell Quenching**
- P. Doornenbal, H. Scheit, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* **111**, 212502 (2013) — 101 citations
- $E(4^+)/E(2^+)$ ratios for ${}^{34,36,38}\text{Mg}$: **3.14(5), 3.07(5), 3.07(5)** — nearly identical and close to the rigid-rotor limit
- Demonstrates persistent strong deformation from N=22 to N=26: the two islands of inversion **merge** in the Mg chain

### 4.4 N=28 Shell Quenching — Deformation in ${}^{42}\text{Si}$

The N=28 magic number (filled $f_{7/2}$ neutron orbital) breaks down in neutron-rich nuclei around Z=14 (silicon).

**${}^{42}\text{Si}$: Well-Developed Deformation**
- S. Takeuchi, M. Matsushita, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* (2012)
- Multi-nucleon removal reactions from ${}^{40}\text{S}$ and ${}^{44}\text{S}$ beams at RIBF
- $E(2^+) = 742(8)$ keV; first identification of $E(4^+) = 2173(14)$ keV
- $E(4^+)/E(2^+) = 2.93(5)$ — indicates well-developed deformation
- Rapid deformation development from N=24 to N=28 in Si isotopes: N=28 shell closure completely breaks down at Z=14

### 4.5 Emergence of N=34 — Evidence from ${}^{54}\text{Ca}$

**${}^{54}\text{Ca}$: New Magic Number** (Aoi's most-cited paper)
- D. Steppenbeck, S. Takeuchi, **N. Aoi** et al., *Nature* **502**, 207 (2013) — **347 citations**
- Proton knockout reactions from ${}^{55}\text{Sc}$ and ${}^{56}\text{Ti}$ beams at RIBF, using [[BigRIPS]] and 186 [[DALI2]] detectors
- The first excited state $(2^+)$ in ${}^{54}\text{Ca}$ lies at a **high energy**, characteristic of a large shell gap
- Direct experimental evidence for a new [[Magic Numbers|magic number]] at N=34, predicted by [[Takaharu Otsuka|Otsuka et al.]] (2001) from the [[Tensor Force|tensor force]] component of the nucleon-nucleon interaction driving the $f_{5/2}$–$p_{1/2}$ gap

### 4.6 Discovery of N=16 Magic Number

**Neutron Drip Line Studies**
- N. Aoi et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* (2000)
- Projectile fragmentation of a 94.1 MeV/u ${}^{40}\text{Ar}$ beam on the [[RIPS]] separator at RIKEN
- First observation of ${}^{31}\text{F}$; evidence for particle instability of ${}^{24,25}\text{N}$, ${}^{27,28}\text{O}$, ${}^{30}\text{F}$
- Established N=16 as a new magic number near the [[Neutron Drip Line|neutron drip line]]

## 5. Nuclear Deformation and Collectivity

### 5.1 Superdeformation and Shape Coexistence in Light Nuclei

[[Superdeformation]] refers to nuclear shapes with axis ratio $\sim 2{:}1$ (quadrupole deformation parameter $\beta_2 \sim 0.6$). [[Shape Coexistence]] occurs when a nucleus has multiple $0^+$ states with very different shapes — spherical, normally deformed, superdeformed — at similar excitation energies.

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: E0 Transitions as a Shape Diagnostic
> **Q:** How do you prove a nucleus has two different shapes coexisting?
> **A:** The smoking gun is the **electric monopole (E0) transition**. Normal gamma decay connects states with different spin ($\Delta J \geq 1$), but E0 transitions connect $0^+ \to 0^+$ states — both have spin zero. A photon cannot carry zero angular momentum, so E0 transitions proceed not by gamma emission but by **internal conversion** (ejecting an atomic electron) or **internal pair creation** (producing an $e^+e^-$ pair).
>
> The strength of the E0 transition, quantified by $\rho^2(E0)$, is proportional to the difference in **mean-square charge radius** between the two $0^+$ states:
>
> $$\rho^2(E0) \propto \left|\langle 0^+_f | \sum_i e_i r_i^2 | 0^+_i \rangle\right|^2$$
>
> If two $0^+$ states have the *same* shape, $\rho^2$ is small. If they have *very different* shapes (say, spherical and superdeformed), $\rho^2$ is large. By measuring E0 strengths, you directly quantify how much the nuclear shape changes between states.
>
> There's a subtlety: if the E0 strength is *anomalously small*, it can mean the two shapes are so different that they barely overlap quantum-mechanically — like two wave functions living in separate potential-energy minima with little tunneling between them. That's exactly what Aoi's group found in ${}^{40}\text{Ca}$: the superdeformed $0^+_3$ state has a tiny $\rho^2$ relative to the ground state, indicating it lives in a deep, isolated potential-energy minimum.

#### E0 Transition from Superdeformed Band in ${}^{40}\text{Ca}$

- E. Ideguchi, T. Kibedi, J.T.H. Dowie, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* **128**, 252501 (2022) — 7 citations
- Technique: $e^+e^-$ pair-conversion spectroscopy using the Super-$e$ pair spectrometer
- $\rho^2(E0; 0^+_3 \to 0^+_1) = 2.3(5) \times 10^{-3}$ — the **smallest** $\rho^2(E0; 0^+ \to 0^+)$ found in $A < 50$ nuclei
- $\rho^2(E0; 0^+_2 \to 0^+_1) = 25.9(16) \times 10^{-3}$ — an order of magnitude larger
- The unusually small E0 strength from the superdeformed $0^+_3$ arises from **destructive interference** in shape-coexisting structures based on multiparticle-multihole excitations
- Large-Scale Shell Model (LSSM) calculations explain the microscopic structure

#### Shape Coexistence and Superdeformation in ${}^{24}\text{Mg}$

- J.T.H. Dowie et al. (N. Aoi co-author), *Phys. Lett. B* **811**, 135855 (2020) — ~15 citations
- First observation of the E0 transition depopulating the first excited $0^+$ state in ${}^{24}\text{Mg}$
- $\rho^2 \times 10^3 = 380(70)$ — a large E0 strength
- Two-state mixing model implies $\beta_2 \sim 1$ for the excited $0^+$ state — **superdeformed**
- Change in mean-square charge radius: $\Delta\langle r^2 \rangle \sim 1.9$ fm$^2$
- Connects to the [[Alpha Cluster|alpha-cluster]] nature of ${}^{24}\text{Mg}$ (six-alpha-particle structure)

#### Search for Hyperdeformed Nuclei

Aoi led a KAKENHI Grant-in-Aid for Scientific Research (A) at [[RCNP|Osaka University]]: **"Search for hyper-deformed nuclei"** (2018–2020). [[Hyperdeformation]] corresponds to axis ratios $\sim 3{:}1$, even more extreme than superdeformation. This remains an active frontier; no definitive observation of hyperdeformed bands has been achieved.

### 5.2 Collectivity at N=40 — Neutron-Rich Cr Isotopes

**${}^{62}\text{Cr}$: Development of Large Deformation** (Aoi first author)
- N. Aoi et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* **102**, 012502 (2009) — 68 citations
- First identification of the $4^+$ state in ${}^{62}\text{Cr}$ at 1180 keV via proton inelastic scattering at RIKEN RIBF
- Rapid increase of $E(4^+)/E(2^+)$ ratio demonstrates large deformation near N=40 — creating what some call a "second island of inversion"

**${}^{74}\text{Ni}$: Enhanced Collectivity**
- N. Aoi et al., *Phys. Lett. B* (2010) — ~55 citations
- Measured inelastic proton scattering on ${}^{74}\text{Ni}$; deformation length $\delta = 1.04(16)$ fm
- Shows enhanced collectivity persists, suggesting weakened $Z = 28$ or $N = 50$ magicity

### 5.3 Neutron-Dominant Collectivity in ${}^{16}\text{C}$

In extremely neutron-rich nuclei, the proton and neutron contributions to collective motion can **decouple**, leading to a qualitatively new form of nuclear collectivity.

**Anomalously Hindered $B(E2)$ in ${}^{16}\text{C}$**
- N. Imai, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. Lett.* **92**, 062501 (2004) — [[Nobuaki_Imai_Research_Program|Imai]] first author
- Measured lifetime of $2^+_1$ state: $77 \pm 14(\text{stat}) \pm 19(\text{syst})$ ps
- $B(E2; 2^+ \to 0^+) = 0.63 \, e^2\text{fm}^4 = 0.26$ W.u. — anomalously small

**Neutron-Dominant Quadrupole Collectivity**
- H.J. Ong, N. Imai, **N. Aoi** et al., *Phys. Rev. C* **73**, 024610 (2006) — 38 citations
- Inelastic proton scattering on ${}^{16}\text{C}$ at 33 MeV/u
- $M_n/M_p = 7.6 \pm 1.7$ — an extraordinarily large ratio indicating the $2^+$ state is nearly a **pure valence neutron excitation**
- The $B(E2; 2^+ \to 0^+) = 0.28 \pm 0.06$ W.u. confirms minimal proton involvement
- Conclusion: the quadrupole collectivity in ${}^{16}\text{C}$ is **neutron-dominant** — protons barely participate in the collective motion, demonstrating proton-neutron decoupling unique to exotic nuclei

### 5.4 B(E2) Systematics in Proton-Rich N=Z Nuclei

- N. Aoi et al., *Eur. Phys. J. A* **25**, s01, 331 (2005)
- Intermediate-energy [[Coulomb Excitation|Coulomb excitation]] at RIKEN
- Measured $B(E2; 0^+ \to 2^+)$ values for ${}^{46}\text{Cr}$, ${}^{50}\text{Fe}$, ${}^{54}\text{Ni}$
- Completed the systematics for $T_z = \pm 1$ even-even pair nuclei up to $Z = 28$
- Double ratios $(|M_n|/|M_p|)/(N/Z)$ extracted and compared with theory, testing [[Isospin Symmetry|isospin symmetry]] of collectivity

## 6. Halo Nuclei and Breakup Reactions

Aoi has been a core contributor to the study of [[Halo Nuclei|halo nuclei]] — systems where one or two weakly-bound valence neutrons extend far beyond the nuclear core, creating an anomalously extended matter distribution.

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: What Is a Nuclear Halo?
> **Q:** What makes a halo nucleus different from a normal nucleus?
> **A:** In a normal nucleus, all the nucleons are packed together in a compact sphere (radius $\sim 1.2 \times A^{1/3}$ fm). A halo nucleus has one or two loosely-bound neutrons that tunnel out into classically forbidden space, forming a dilute "cloud" that extends far beyond the core. The poster child is ${}^{11}\text{Li}$: its core (${}^{9}\text{Li}$) has a radius of about 2.3 fm, but the two halo neutrons extend the matter radius to about 3.5 fm — giving it a matter radius comparable to ${}^{48}\text{Ca}$, despite having only 11 nucleons.
>
> The experimental signature is a **large reaction cross section** (the nucleus appears "bigger" than expected), strong **low-energy E1 strength** (the core and halo oscillate against each other like a dipole), and characteristic **narrow momentum distributions** in breakup products (reflecting the large spatial extent via the uncertainty principle).

### Key Results

| Nucleus | Measurement | Key Result | Citation | Year |
|---------|------------|------------|----------|------|
| ${}^{11}\text{Be}$ | [[Coulomb Dissociation]] at 72 MeV/u | Confirmed halo structure; $S = 0.72 \pm 0.04$ | 236 cit. | 1994 |
| ${}^{19}\text{C}$ | Coulomb dissociation | Established one-neutron halo character | 235 cit. | 1999 |
| ${}^{11}\text{Li}$ | Coulomb dissociation at 70 MeV/u | Strong low-energy E1: $B(E1) = 1.42(18) \, e^2\text{fm}^2$ at $E_x \sim 0.6$ MeV | 254 cit. | 2006 |
| ${}^{31}\text{Ne}$ | Coulomb breakup | Halo in the [[Island of Inversion]]; deformation-halo interplay | 211 cit. | 2009 |

| ${}^{11}\text{Be}$ | Coulomb + nuclear breakup | Detailed study separating Coulomb and nuclear mechanisms | 220 cit. | 2004 |

Several of these papers are among Aoi's highest-cited works. The halo research program was conducted primarily at RIKEN using the [[RIPS]] (older) and [[BigRIPS]] (newer) fragment separators.

### 7.1 DALI2 Gamma-Ray Array

### 7.2 CAT / CAT-M Active-Target TPC

The **[[CAT-M]]** (*CNS Active Target — Medium*) is a [[GEM Detector|GEM]]-based [[Active-Target TPC]] developed at [[UTokyo CNS]], primarily by [[Shinsuke Ota]] (now at [[RCNP]]) and the NUSPEQ group.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Active area | $\sim 280 \times 310$ mm$^2$ |
| Amplification | Multi-layered thick GEM (1.2 mm) |
| Gain design | Dual-gain: low gain (beam region), high gain (recoil region) |
| Rate capability | $> 10^5$ Hz |

> [!Feynman]- Feynman: Why an Active Target?
> **Q:** What is an "active target" and why use one?
> **A:** In a conventional experiment, the beam hits a separate **target** (e.g., a foil of beryllium) and then reaction products fly into **detectors** placed around it. The target and detector are different objects. In an **active-target TPC**, the detector gas itself *is* the target. The beam enters the gas-filled volume, reacts with gas nuclei (e.g., deuterium, helium-4), and the recoiling particles ionize the same gas. The ionization electrons drift to a readout plane, providing a 3D image of the reaction.
>
> Why is this useful? Three reasons: (1) You get **thick-target luminosity** (lots of target atoms) without losing energy resolution, because you reconstruct the reaction vertex along the beam path. (2) You detect **low-energy recoils** that would stop inside a conventional solid target before reaching external detectors. (3) You cover **nearly $4\pi$** solid angle for recoil particles. The challenge is the enormous dynamic range: the beam deposits orders of magnitude more ionization than the recoils, which is why CAT-M uses a dual-gain GEM design — low amplification in the beam corridor, high amplification everywhere else.

The **CAT-S** (*CNS Active Target — Small*, $\sim 100 \times 100$ mm$^2$) is a companion smaller detector.

The CNS thick-GEM technology, developed in this program, achieves higher gain at lower voltage than original CERN designs and was adopted in the [[ALICE]] TPC upgrade at [[CERN]].

Physics applications include: [[Missing-Mass Spectroscopy|missing-mass spectroscopy]], nuclear [[Incompressibility|incompressibility]] (ISGMR), and [[Deuteron|deuteron]] inelastic scattering.

**Connection to DONUTS group:** The DG-M-THGEM prototype described in Imai's research program (Iwamoto, Ota, Imai et al., *PTEP* 2023) is closely related technology, and the CAT-M is shared infrastructure between the NUSPEQ and [[DONUTS]] groups — both using it with the [[OEDO]]-[[SHARAQ Spectrometer|SHARAQ]] system.

### 7.3 CAGRA

**[[CAGRA]]** (*Clover Array Gamma-ray spectrometer at RCNP for Advanced research*) is a 16-element Compton-suppressed Ge Clover array constructed as a U.S.-Japan collaboration. Aoi was involved in its development during his time at [[RCNP]]. It is used for high-resolution nuclear structure studies at both RCNP and RIBF.

### 7.4 Other Detector Systems

- **Super-$e$ Pair Spectrometer** — used for $e^+e^-$ pair-conversion spectroscopy in the ${}^{40}\text{Ca}$ and ${}^{24}\text{Mg}$ E0 transition measurements
- **[[TiNA]]** — Silicon strip + CsI(Tl) recoil detector array ($> 1000$ channels, $< 100$ keV energy resolution); used for neutron capture reaction studies
- **[[PANDORA]]** — Neutron counter array ($> 100$ keV threshold); plastic scintillator with pulse-shape discrimination
- **Spin-controlled RI beams** — Aoi co-authored the *Nature Physics* (2012) paper on producing RI beams with controlled nuclear spin polarization
